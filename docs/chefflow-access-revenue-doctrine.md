# ChefFlow Access And Revenue Doctrine

Status: canonical monetization doctrine
Last updated: 2026-04-30
Owner: Founder Authority

## Purpose

This document defines what ChefFlow may charge for, what it must not charge for, and what proof is required before monetization expands beyond voluntary support.

If this document conflicts with older Pro, tier, support, or marketplace language, this document wins for access and monetization philosophy.

## Core Rule

ChefFlow will never charge a chef simply to price a menu with honest local pricing data.

Menu pricing is baseline infrastructure. A chef should not have to pay before they can understand what a menu costs in their region.

This rule includes:

- entering or selecting a menu
- pricing that menu from available local data
- seeing real prices separately from estimated prices
- seeing source, freshness, confidence, and fallback labels
- understanding whether the price is local, regional, estimated, stale, or incomplete

ChefFlow may charge for advanced leverage after the menu is priced. It must not charge for the basic right to price the menu honestly.

## Product Identity

ChefFlow is an operating system first.

It should have marketplace-capable infrastructure from the beginning, but marketplace is not the first identity and should not become an extractive lead gate.

ChefFlow should help chefs operate better first. Discovery, marketplace, ads, vendors, and paid partner surfaces can grow from that foundation later.

## Free Core Promise

These belong in the free foundation:

- account and chef profile
- public chef page
- inquiry intake
- client basics
- menu creation
- recipe library ownership
- event creation and lifecycle
- quote creation
- basic documents and PDFs
- basic calendar
- manual messaging
- manual payment recording
- ledger foundation
- basic price lookup
- honest local menu pricing
- real vs estimated price labels
- source, freshness, confidence, and fallback visibility
- data export
- Stripe Connect onboarding
- ability to collect payments, subject to transparent transaction or platform fees

Free does not mean low quality. Free means the operating foundation is accessible.

## Paid Leverage

Paid offerings should begin where ChefFlow adds automation, scale, expensive infrastructure, or business leverage beyond the baseline.

Good paid candidates:

- automatic menu price refresh
- historical price trends
- margin simulation
- vendor substitution strategy
- procurement plans
- client-ready proposal automation
- quote optimization across multiple menus
- advanced analytics
- team and staff workflows
- inventory automation
- commerce and POS
- SMS and high-cost messaging bundles
- advanced integrations and API access
- compliance modules
- cannabis dining operations
- multi-location command center
- white-label or custom domain surfaces
- concierge onboarding, migration, or done-for-you setup

Bad paid candidates:

- blocking a chef from pricing a menu
- hiding basic client or event workflows
- forcing payment to access the chef's own operational data
- charging for fake scarcity
- charging for low-quality leads
- turning public discovery into a toll booth that owns the chef-client relationship

## Payment Revenue

ChefFlow may earn revenue when it helps money move, provided the fee is transparent.

Acceptable payment revenue:

- platform fee on payments collected through ChefFlow
- fixed transaction fee
- ticketed event buyer fee
- gift card purchaser fee
- instant payout fee, if clearly optional
- group payment or split-payment fee

Payment revenue must not obscure Stripe fees, platform fees, payout timing, refund behavior, or who receives the money.

## Marketplace Doctrine

Marketplace infrastructure is allowed from day one.

Marketplace identity should emerge from operational excellence, not from gatekeeping demand.

ChefFlow may support:

- public chef discovery
- chef profiles
- local service pages
- inquiry routing
- client booking flows
- public events
- gift cards
- buyer-facing FAQs and trust surfaces
- optional featured placement with clear labeling

ChefFlow must avoid:

- pretending to be the chef's only source of clients
- taking control of the chef-client relationship as the core business model
- hiding client information only to extract a fee
- charging for unqualified or low-quality leads
- copying extractive marketplace behavior under a friendlier brand

## Ads And Partner Ethics

Ads are allowed only when they are useful, relevant, and clearly labeled.

Acceptable ad and sponsorship surfaces:

- sponsored supplier listings
- sponsored ingredient results
- vendor discovery
- equipment, insurance, payroll, accounting, or education partners
- public marketplace placement
- newsletter sponsorships
- educational sponsorships
- clearly labeled featured chef or event placement

Banned or high-risk ad surfaces:

- private chef workspaces where ads distract from operational work
- ads that look like system recommendations
- ads that corrupt price truth
- ads based on selling private chef or client data
- sponsored placement without clear disclosure

Ad disclosure must use direct language such as "Sponsored" or "Ad."

## Pricing Truth Standard

ChefFlow pricing must distinguish real data from estimated data.

Every priced result should be able to explain:

- source
- geography
- radius
- freshness
- confidence
- fallback path
- whether the value is real or estimated

Estimated values are allowed only when the system has enough supporting evidence to make the estimate honest. Estimates should never be displayed as real prices.

## Radius Standard

ChefFlow should not assume one universal pricing radius for the entire United States.

The radius should be evidence-driven. Some regions may have stable pricing across a wide area. Other regions may shift quickly across a shorter distance.

The pricing engine should learn where price behavior changes enough to affect menu costing, then choose or explain a sensible radius for that chef's ZIP code or service area.

## Founder Confidence Gate

ChefFlow should not aggressively monetize core-adjacent pricing leverage until the product passes a confidence gate.

The gate is met when ChefFlow can repeatedly prove:

- a chef can enter a ZIP code anywhere in the United States
- the system selects a sensible local or regional pricing radius
- menu pricing changes when local data meaningfully changes
- real and estimated prices are visibly different
- estimates are backed by a defined confidence threshold
- source, freshness, confidence, and fallback are visible or explainable
- 100 or more chef accounts can price realistic menus across varied regions without hand-holding
- failures show honest degraded states instead of fake certainty

Playwright and Codex workflow tests can prove the app flow. They do not prove pricing truth by themselves. Pricing truth requires data evidence, source evidence, freshness evidence, and confidence evidence.

## Website And FAQ Positioning

Public copy may say:

- ChefFlow is an operating system for chefs before it is a marketplace.
- Core chef infrastructure should be accessible.
- A chef should not need fake grocery carts or spreadsheets to price a menu.
- Monetization comes after reliable utility.
- ChefFlow is chef-led and built from lived private chef experience.
- ChefFlow can work alongside existing demand sources while giving chefs their own operational foundation.

Public copy should not attack competitors by name. Competitor critique belongs in internal research and product strategy.

## Maintenance Rule

Any future monetization build must first state whether it affects:

- free core
- paid leverage
- payment revenue
- marketplace revenue
- ads or sponsorship
- partner revenue
- data or benchmark revenue

If a proposed feature charges for free core, it is blocked unless Founder Authority explicitly changes this doctrine.
