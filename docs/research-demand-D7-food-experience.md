# Research: Give Me a Food Experience (D7)

Date: 2026-03-14
Source: ChefFlow demand-side research
Status: Complete

> Market research conducted 2026-03-14 across Reddit, travel forums, Trustpilot, Yelp, Tripadvisor, industry reports, academic papers, and culinary experience platforms.

---

## Executive Summary

- The "food experience" need-state is driven by people who want dining to be an event, not just a meal. This covers supper clubs, cooking classes, chef's table experiences, food tours, pop-up restaurants, and experiential dining gifts. The US culinary tourism market alone was valued at $2.7 billion in 2024 and is projected to hit $7.6 billion by 2030 (Grand View Research).
- Discovery is the #1 pain point. There is no single trusted marketplace for food experiences. Consumers bounce between Eventbrite, Instagram, EatWith, Cozymeal, Airbnb Experiences, Resy, word-of-mouth, and Google, and none of these platforms serve the full spectrum well.
- Willingness to pay is high: 75% of guests will pay more for exclusive, personalized experiences (NRA data). Cooking classes run $59-$195/person, chef's table dinners $150-$500+/person, supper clubs $50-$150/person, food tours $60-$120/person.
- The experience economy is accelerating. 42% of Americans report more interest in experiential dining in 2025 vs 2024, with bookings for tasting menus and cooking classes up 27% year-over-year (Eventbrite/OpenTable data).
- Trust and legitimacy are the biggest barriers. Underground dining raises food safety and legal questions. Platforms like Airbnb Experiences and EatWith have quality consistency issues. The market needs a curated, trusted middle ground between "sketchy underground dinner" and "overpriced corporate cooking class."

---

## 1. The Trigger

**What makes someone enter this need-state?**

People do not wake up searching for "food experiences." Something triggers the shift from "I need to eat" to "I want something memorable." The evidence points to five primary triggers:

**Trigger 1: A special occasion that demands more than a restaurant reservation.**
Anniversaries, birthdays, proposals, milestone celebrations. The standard "nice dinner out" feels insufficient. A couple celebrating their 10th anniversary doesn't want another table at a steakhouse. They want a private chef's table, a cooking class together, or a secret supper club. The occasion elevates the need.

**Trigger 2: Social media exposure to experiential dining.**
Someone sees a friend's Instagram story from a rooftop pop-up dinner or a TikTok of a private cooking class and thinks "I want that." 72% of diners in a Technomic study said they wanted more experiential dining options after seeing them online, including chef's tables, themed events, and interactive culinary experiences.

**Trigger 3: Travel and tourism.**
Food tours and cooking classes are among the fastest-growing segments of culinary tourism. 81% of travelers prioritize exploring local food as the highlight of their trips (Grand View Research). Travelers search for authentic food experiences in new cities, and many discover they want similar experiences at home afterward.

**Trigger 4: Gift-giving frustration.**
"What do you get the person who has everything?" Experience gifts have become the default answer for affluent demographics. Food experiences (cooking classes, chef's table dinners, food tours) solve the gift problem elegantly. Platforms like Xperience Days, Giftory, and Tinggly have built entire businesses around this.

**Trigger 5: Boredom with traditional dining.**
Repeat restaurant-goers hit a ceiling. Every restaurant starts to feel the same. They crave novelty: eating in someone's home, cooking alongside a chef, discovering hidden neighborhood gems on a walking tour. Gen Z and Millennials in particular are driving this: 71% of Gen Z and 68% of Millennials planned to dine out more in 2025, but they want experiences, not just meals (Toast data).

## 2. The Search Journey

**Step by step, what do they do?**

The search journey for food experiences is fragmented and frustrating. There is no single "Expedia for food experiences." Here is the typical path:

**Step 1: Vague Google search.**
"Unique dining experience near me," "cooking classes [city]," "fun dinner ideas for couples," "things to do this weekend [city]." Google returns a mix of listicles, Yelp pages, Eventbrite events, and platform ads (Cozymeal, Classpop). The consumer is immediately overwhelmed.

**Step 2: Platform browsing.**
They visit 2-3 platforms. Common paths:

- Cooking classes: Cozymeal, Classpop, Sur La Table, local cooking school websites
- Supper clubs / pop-ups: Eventbrite, Instagram hashtags, local food blogs, word-of-mouth
- Chef's table: OpenTable (filters for "chef's table"), Resy (events section), restaurant websites directly
- Food tours: Viator, GetYourGuide, Airbnb Experiences, TripAdvisor
- Underground dining: SecretEATS, Instagram DMs, email lists, Reddit threads, Facebook groups

**Step 3: Social validation.**
Before booking, they search for reviews. "[Platform/experience name] reviews reddit," "[experience name] worth it," "[experience name] tripadvisor." Trust is thin because many of these experiences are small-batch, low-review-count operations. A supper club with 3 reviews feels risky. A cooking class with 200 five-star reviews on Cozymeal feels safer but potentially generic.

**Step 4: Price comparison and sticker shock.**
They compare the experience price to what they would spend at a restaurant. A $150/person supper club or $195/couple cooking class triggers "is this really worth it?" calculations. The experience must clearly justify the premium over a standard dinner out.

**Step 5: Booking (or abandonment).**
If the experience passes the trust, price, and logistics tests, they book. Many abandon at this stage due to: inconvenient dates, confusing booking flows, unclear what's included (wine? tax? tip?), group size minimums, or simply not finding something that matches what they imagined.

## 3. Where It Breaks

**Platform by platform, what fails?**

**Eventbrite**

- Good for discovering one-off events and pop-ups, but quality varies wildly. No curation. A $25 "cooking class" might be someone heating up pre-made ingredients in their apartment.
- Attendance at cooking classes up 33% over 2019 levels (Eventbrite Trends Report), but the platform does not distinguish between professional and amateur hosts.
- Bar crawls and "boozy brunch" events up 82% attendance year-over-year, diluting the signal for serious food experiences.

**Airbnb Experiences**

- Hosts cannot set a minimum guest count, meaning they may have to run a class for one person at a loss.
- "How to spot fake 'local experience' tours disguised as cooking classes" is a real concern, per multiple review aggregators.
- Guests report: "kitchen felt commercial," "ingredients came pre-packaged," "no interaction with the host's family" as signs of inauthentic experiences.
- Airbnb's review and approval process for new experiences is opaque and inconsistent.

**EatWith**

- Adds 20-30% markup on top of host pricing. If a host charges $60/guest, the guest pays $78-$84. This makes prices feel inflated compared to restaurant dining.
- Business hours do not cover evenings or weekends, when most events occur. No customer support when problems happen during dinner.
- Events cancelled day-of with no notice or compensation reported on Trustpilot.
- App freezes and technical bugs reported.
- Average rating 4.9 on Yelp (69 reviews), but individual complaint threads reveal serious operational gaps.

**Cozymeal**

- Average rating 4.6 from 1,124 reviews. Generally positive but complaints about promotional gift vouchers that restrict usage to "signature" classes at $55 each, with remaining voucher value forfeited.
- Home visit availability issues reported in multiple markets.
- Strong in cooking classes, weak in other food experience categories.

**Classpop**

- Complaints about misleading descriptions: guests expected hands-on cooking, arrived to find a demonstration where they sat behind a table watching someone cook.
- Promotional gift vouchers restrict usage, confusing customers.
- "Slow, boring, and not interactive" reported when execution does not match marketing.

**Resy / OpenTable**

- Strong for restaurant reservations, including chef's table and tasting menu bookings at established restaurants.
- Resy's "Global Dining Access" offers exclusive events, but only for American Express cardholders.
- Neither platform serves the private/home-based experience market at all.
- Pop-ups and supper clubs are invisible on these platforms.

**SecretEATS**

- Curated, high-quality, but very small scale: 300+ events since 2013, limited to 10 guests per dinner.
- Only operates in a handful of cities (Chicago, London).
- Not a marketplace. More of a bespoke concierge service.

**Instagram / Word of Mouth**

- The primary discovery channel for underground dining, supper clubs, and pop-ups. But it is unreliable: accounts go dark, events sell out before you see the post, no booking infrastructure, no reviews, no accountability.
- "The underground scene relies heavily on word-of-mouth. Joining local Reddit threads or following niche hashtags on Instagram can also lead you to secret mailing lists." This is the state of the art for discovery, and it is terrible.

## 4. The Decision Factors

**What matters most?**

Evidence from reviews, forums, and survey data points to these factors, ranked by importance:

**1. Authenticity.**
The #1 decision factor. Consumers in this need-state are explicitly seeking something different from a restaurant. If the experience feels corporate, scripted, or mass-produced, it fails. "Every dinner you go to is completely different" is the aspiration. Pre-packaged ingredients, commercial kitchens disguised as homes, and scripted patter are deal-breakers.

**2. The host/chef.**
The personality, skill, and story of the person leading the experience matters more than the venue or menu. Successful supper clubs and cooking classes build followings around specific chefs. This is personality-driven commerce. A charismatic chef teaching pasta-making in their actual kitchen will outsell a polished cooking school with hired instructors.

**3. Social proof (reviews and photos).**
Low review counts are a barrier. High review counts with specific details (not just "great experience!") build trust. Photos from past events are critical. Consumer forums consistently show people searching for reviews before booking any food experience.

**4. Price transparency.**
"What's included?" is the most common pre-booking question. Does the price include wine? Is tax and tip extra? Are ingredients provided or do I need to bring something? Platforms that bury fees or add 20-30% markups (like EatWith) create resentment even when the experience itself is good.

**5. Group size and intimacy.**
Smaller is better for this audience. SecretEATS caps at 10. The best supper clubs seat 8-20. The best cooking classes run 6-12. When group sizes creep above 20, the experience becomes a "class" or "event" and loses the intimate, personal quality that drives the need-state.

**6. Logistics and convenience.**
Date availability, location accessibility, parking, dietary accommodation, cancellation policy. These are table stakes, not differentiators, but failures here kill the booking.

## 5. The Constraints That Matter Most

**Which constraint dimensions have the biggest impact?**

**Constraint 1: Legality and regulation.**
Underground dining operates in a legal gray zone. In most US jurisdictions, selling food from a non-commercial kitchen without permits violates health codes. Fines can reach $2,000+ per violation (CBS New York investigation). California's AB 626 (2019) created Microenterprise Home Kitchen Operations (MEHKOs), allowing home-based food sales up to $100,000/year, 30 meals/day, 90 meals/week, but only in counties that have opted in. Most states have no equivalent. This constraint pushes the best supper club chefs into illegal operation or forces them into expensive commercial kitchen rentals that undermine the intimate home-dining value proposition.

**Constraint 2: Scalability vs. authenticity tension.**
The experiences that consumers value most (small, intimate, personal) are inherently unscalable. A chef running a 10-seat supper club twice a month in their home generates roughly $3,000-$6,000/month at $150/person. That is viable as a side hustle, not a full-time income, unless prices go significantly higher.

**Constraint 3: Discovery fragmentation.**
No single platform aggregates food experiences across categories. A consumer looking for "something special to do this Saturday" has to search Eventbrite, Instagram, Cozymeal, OpenTable, Resy, Google, and ask friends. The search cost is high enough that many people default to "let's just go to a nice restaurant."

**Constraint 4: Insurance and liability.**
Home-based food experiences carry liability risk. EatWith reportedly offered hosts up to $1 million in Cook Protection insurance. Feastly did the same. Most independent supper club hosts have no coverage. One foodborne illness incident could result in a lawsuit that ends the operation. This constraint keeps many talented chefs from hosting.

**Constraint 5: Seasonality and scheduling.**
Food experiences are heavily seasonal. Holiday periods (November-January), Valentine's Day, and summer weekends drive demand spikes. Midweek January events are hard to fill. This creates inconsistent revenue for hosts and inconsistent availability for consumers.

## 6. The Trust Barrier

**What are they afraid of? What would make them trust a new platform?**

**Fear 1: Food safety.**
"Underground" and "secret" are exciting marketing terms, but they also trigger food safety anxiety. "Food safety concerns are real, especially because many secret suppers involve 'illegal' and uninspected foods: raw milks and cheese, cured meats, and foraged sea creatures" (University of Chicago Law Review). Consumers want adventure, but not at the cost of getting sick.

**Fear 2: Wasting money on a bad experience.**
At $100-$200/person, a disappointing food experience is an expensive mistake. Unlike a restaurant where you can check Yelp reviews from 500 people, many food experiences have thin review profiles. The risk-reward calculation is unfavorable for first-time bookings.

**Fear 3: Social awkwardness.**
Supper clubs and communal dining involve sitting with strangers. Introverts and socially anxious consumers find this intimidating. "What if the other guests are weird? What if I'm the youngest/oldest person there? What if it's cliquey?"

**Fear 4: Logistical uncertainty.**
"Where exactly am I going? Is it safe? Will I know where to park? What if I have dietary restrictions they can't accommodate? What happens if the host cancels?"

**What would build trust:**

- Verified chef credentials and food safety certifications displayed prominently
- Photo and video from past events (not stock photos)
- Reviews from verified attendees with specific detail
- Clear cancellation and refund policies
- Transparent pricing (all-inclusive, no hidden fees)
- Host response rate and booking history visible
- Dietary accommodation capabilities listed upfront
- Insurance/liability coverage disclosed

**Market gap:** No platform serves as the trusted marketplace across all food experience categories (supper clubs + cooking classes + chef's table + food tours + pop-ups + gifting). Each platform owns one or two segments poorly. The full spectrum is unserved.

**Failed platforms:**

- **Feastly** (founded 2013): Marketplace for supper clubs and home-cooked meals. Offered $1M Cook Protection insurance. Operated in multiple cities. Quietly shut down. The home-dining marketplace model has been attempted and has failed before, primarily due to regulatory friction, quality control challenges, and thin margins after platform fees.
- The Feastly failure is instructive: the demand is real, but the supply-side economics (small batches, legal ambiguity, high operational overhead per event) make it difficult to build a venture-scale marketplace.

## 8. The Willingness to Pay

**How do they think about money?**

This audience does not think about food experiences the way they think about restaurant meals. The mental model is "entertainment budget" or "experience budget," not "dinner budget."

**Price ranges by category (US market, 2025):**

| Experience Type                         | Low End     | Mid Range        | High End     | What's Included                                        |
| --------------------------------------- | ----------- | ---------------- | ------------ | ------------------------------------------------------ |
| Cooking class (group)                   | $59/person  | $100-$130/person | $195+/person | Instruction, ingredients, meal, sometimes wine         |
| Cooking class (couples/date night)      | $70/couple  | $130-$150/couple | $250+/couple | Same as above, more intimate setting                   |
| Cooking class (corporate team building) | $90/person  | $130-$149/person | $300+/person | Private kitchen, instruction, meal, event coordination |
| Supper club / pop-up dinner             | $40/person  | $75-$100/person  | $150+/person | Multi-course meal, sometimes wine, the "experience"    |
| Chef's table experience                 | $150/person | $185-$250/person | $495+/person | Multi-course tasting, wine pairings, kitchen access    |
| Food tour (walking)                     | $50/person  | $75-$90/person   | $120+/person | 3-4 hours, multiple stops, tastings, guide             |
| Private dining (at-home chef)           | $55/person  | $100-$150/person | $200+/person | Shopping, cooking, service, cleanup                    |
| Experience gift packages                | $25         | $75-$150         | $500+        | Gift card/voucher for any of the above                 |

**Key insight on price psychology:**

- 64% of full-service diners value the overall experience more than price (National Restaurant Association).
- 75% of guests will pay more for exclusive, personalized experiences.
- 42% of Americans are more interested in experiential dining in 2025 vs 2024.
- Nearly 75% of consumers find multi-course tasting menus, live entertainment, or culturally specific themes "very or extremely appealing."
- 62% would pay more for better ambiance at dinner.

**The price comparison consumers make:**
They compare the food experience not to grocery costs or fast food, but to "what else could I do with $200 for a date night?" A $150/person chef's table competes with concert tickets + dinner, a spa day, a weekend getaway. In that frame, food experiences are often well-priced.

## 9. The Retention Hook

**What turns a one-time search into a repeat user?**

**Hook 1: The chef/host relationship.**
When a guest connects with a specific chef, they follow that chef. They sign up for the mailing list. They attend the next supper club. They book a private dinner. The relationship is the retention engine, not the platform. This is why Instagram works for underground dining: it keeps you connected to the person, not the venue.

**Hook 2: Community belonging.**
Supper clubs that cultivate a "club" identity (regulars, inside jokes, traditions) create powerful retention. "Guests of the underground restaurant cite one of the biggest reasons for enjoying the experience is the social interaction with strangers over food, something which would generally be frowned upon in a traditional restaurant setting." The social bond becomes the product.

**Hook 3: Novelty rotation.**
The best food experience operations change their menu, theme, or format every event. There is always something new to try. A cooking class studio that offers Italian one month, Thai the next, and sushi the month after keeps customers returning because they haven't "done it" yet.

**Hook 4: Gifting loops.**
Someone receives a cooking class as a gift, loves it, and then buys the same experience as a gift for someone else. The experience-as-gift flywheel is documented across Cozymeal, Classpop, and Xperience Days.

**Hook 5: Seasonal/calendar anchoring.**
"We always do a supper club for Valentine's Day." "We always take a cooking class on our anniversary." When an experience becomes part of someone's annual tradition, retention becomes automatic.

**What kills retention:**

- Inconsistent quality between events
- Chef/host turnover (the person they bonded with is gone)
- Price increases without corresponding value increase
- Platform friction (hard to rebook, confusing gift vouchers, cancellation hassles)
- Expanding group sizes to fill seats, diluting intimacy

Restaurant loyalty data for context: 70% of first-time diners never return. Loyal customers spend 25-95% more. Loyalty program members visit 20% more frequently and spend 20% more per visit. These dynamics apply even more strongly to food experiences, where the emotional investment is higher.

## 10. The Unmet Need

**What would they use if it existed?**

Based on the evidence across all sources, the unmet need is a **curated food experience marketplace** that solves the trust-discovery-booking gap. Specifically:

**Need 1: A single place to discover all types of food experiences.**
Not just cooking classes (Cozymeal). Not just restaurant reservations (Resy). Not just tourist food tours (Viator). A unified platform where someone can search "what food experiences are happening in [my city] this weekend?" and see supper clubs, pop-ups, cooking classes, chef's tables, food tours, and private dining options in one place, with consistent quality curation.

**Need 2: Chef-centric profiles that build trust.**
The chef is the product. A platform that gives chefs rich profiles (bio, credentials, food safety certs, past event photos, reviews, style/philosophy) lets consumers build trust in the person, not just the event. This is what Instagram does informally. A proper platform would do it with structure, reviews, and booking infrastructure.

**Need 3: Flexible format support.**
A chef might want to host a 10-person supper club in their home on Saturday, teach a couples cooking class on Wednesday, and do a private chef dinner for a client on Friday. Current platforms force chefs to pick one format. The unmet need is a platform that supports all formats from one chef profile.

**Need 4: Built-in compliance and insurance.**
The legal ambiguity of home-based food experiences stops many talented chefs from hosting. A platform that provides (or facilitates) food safety certification, event insurance, and regulatory guidance by jurisdiction would unlock significant supply. California's MEHKO model shows this can work when the infrastructure exists.

**Need 5: Experience gifting that actually works.**
Current gifting options are either generic gift cards (Cozymeal, Classpop) with restrictive terms, or platform-agnostic experience vouchers (Tinggly, Xperience Days) that are too broad. The ideal is a gifting system where you can buy a specific experience (or let the recipient choose from curated options), with no hidden restrictions, no expiration traps, and clear redemption.

**Need 6: Revenue tools for chefs who host experiences.**
Most supper club hosts and cooking class instructors use spreadsheets, Venmo, and Instagram DMs to run their business. They need: event creation, ticketing, guest communication, dietary/allergy collection, payment processing, waitlists, CRM (who came, what they liked, when to invite them back), and financial tracking. All in one place.
