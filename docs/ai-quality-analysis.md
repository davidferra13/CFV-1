## What ChefFlow Does Well

### 1. Privacy Architecture — Genuinely Rare

The hard wall between local AI (Ollama) and cloud AI (Gemini) is architecturally sound and enforced at the code level. `OllamaOfflineError` is re-thrown up the stack, preventing silent fallbacks. Most products claim privacy; this one actually builds the circuit breaker. This is a real differentiator if marketed correctly.

### 2. Financial Model — Ledger-First Is the Right Call

Storing financials as immutable ledger entries with computed views (`event_financial_summary`) eliminates an entire class of bugs — balance drift, double-counting, corrupted totals. The DB-level immutability trigger means no application bug can break the books. This is how accounting software is supposed to work, and most startups don't do it.

### 3. Event State Machine — Clean and Enforceable

Eight states, explicit transitions, database-enforced. The FSM makes it impossible for an event to skip states or exist in an undefined status. The `event_transitions` table with its immutability trigger gives a full audit trail. This is production-grade.

### 4. Multi-Tenancy — Consistently Scoped

`tenant_id` on every query, derived from session (never from request body), enforced in server actions. No accidental data leakage between chefs. The test coverage for this is solid.

### 5. Automation Infrastructure — Breadth Is Impressive

20 scheduled cron routes covering: inquiry expiry, quote expiry, event reminders, payment reminders, birthday sequences, holiday campaigns, review requests, Gmail sync, calendar sync, lead scoring, goal tracking. For a solo-built product this is exceptional breadth.

### 6. Simulation Lab — Quality Loop Exists

Very few products have a built-in AI quality testing loop. The simulation lab generates synthetic scenarios, runs them through real prompts, grades outputs, collects fine-tuning examples, and surfaces failure patterns. This is sophisticated. Most products ship AI features and hope they work.

### 7. Day-of-Event Operational Depth

The DOP (Day-of Protocol) with temp logs, staff briefings, contingency panels, and the 16-item terminal checklist shows deep understanding of actual private chef work. This isn't a generic booking platform — it's built by someone who knows the job.

## What Needs Improvement

### 1. AI Modules Don't Self-Correct (Yet)

**The gap:** The simulation lab identifies failures with specific reasons (e.g., "allergen_risk: missed 'tree nuts' despite 'nut allergy' in text"). But there's no mechanism to feed those failures back into improved prompts. The loop collects data but doesn't close.

**What to build:** After each simulation run, pass the failure `failures[]` arrays to Ollama and ask it to suggest prompt revisions. Surface these suggestions in the Simulation Lab UI under "Prompt Improvement Suggestions." The chef/dev approves and applies them. This is the actual closed loop.

### 2. Mobile UX Is Desktop-First

**The gap:** Most panels (grocery quote, temp logs, event detail, recipe sprint) are built for wide screens. The private chef often needs to check things mid-service on a phone. The mobile nav exists but many key flows are hard to use one-handed.

**What to build:** Prioritize the three DOP flows for mobile: (a) temp log quick entry, (b) receipt photo upload, (c) event checklist tick-off. These happen in the kitchen. Everything else can be desktop-only.

### 3. Prompt Quality Is Not Visible to the Chef

**The gap:** The Simulation Lab is admin-only, hidden behind `/dev/simulate`. The chef never sees that Allergen Risk detection has been passing at 65% all month — they just assume AI works.

**What to build:** A small "AI Health" indicator in the chef dashboard sidebar — a simple green/amber/red dot with a tooltip: "Inquiry parsing: 89% | Allergen detection: 65% (needs attention)." No numbers in the main view; just the signal. This gives the chef visibility without overwhelming them.

### 4. Revenue Reports Are Data-Rich but Not Insight-Rich

**The gap:** The finance pages show ledger entries, payment summaries, and goal tracking. But there's no "so what" — no narrative. A chef ending June at 73% of goal doesn't know if they should be worried or whether their historical pattern makes this normal.

**What to build:** Monthly narrative cards generated by Ollama from the chef's own historical data (fully local — no private data leaves). "You hit 73% of your June goal. In previous years you've averaged 68% through June and finished at 104% by year-end. Your two strongest months are historically September and October." This is the kind of insight that turns a number into an action.

### 5. Inquiry Pipeline Has No Confidence Score

**The gap:** Inquiries come in, get parsed, and land in the pipeline. The chef sees structured data (guest count, occasion, budget) but has no signal on how confident the AI was in parsing it.

**What to build:** A small confidence badge on each inquiry: "High confidence" (green) / "Review parsing" (amber). Amber means the AI wasn't sure — the chef should manually verify before quoting. This prevents misquotes from bad parses.

### 6. Recipe Costing Is Manual-Heavy

**The gap:** The recipe capture flow is good, but ingredient prices need to be fetched and confirmed for each recipe individually. In a real kitchen, prices change weekly. There's no "re-price everything for this week" flow.

**What to build:** A "Weekly Market Update" action on the grocery quote page: pull current prices for all ingredients used in events scheduled in the next 14 days, update cost estimates, flag recipes where food cost % has shifted by more than 5 points. This takes 30 seconds and gives the chef accurate margins before they commit.

### 7. Client Communication Is Logged, Not Drafted

**The gap:** The system logs communication but the AI drafting is buried in the inquiry flow. There's no "draft a follow-up for this client" quick action from the client card.

**What to build:** On every client card and every event detail page, add a "Draft Message" button that opens a quick context panel: "What's the occasion?" → Ollama drafts a message in the chef's voice → chef edits and approves → logs automatically. This is the highest-ROI UX change for day-to-day chef workflow.

### 8. Onboarding Is Thin

**The gap:** New chefs land in the dashboard with demo data but no guided experience. The feature set is dense and the value isn't obvious without a tour.

**What to build:** A 5-step onboarding checklist (shown once, dismissible): (1) Add your first real client, (2) Send your first quote, (3) Connect Gmail, (4) Set a revenue goal, (5) Run your first simulation. Each step links directly to the right page. Progress persists. Completion rate is the key metric for activation.

### 9. Push Dinners / Campaign Flow Is New and Untested

**The gap:** The push dinner campaign system (`lib/campaigns/`, `/marketing/push-dinners/`) is recently added but lacks feedback loops — no tracking of who opened, who booked, what converted.

**What to build:** After a campaign runs, auto-generate a simple results card: X emails sent, Y opened (if Gmail tracking is connected), Z booked. Even without open tracking, booking attribution (did a booking come within 14 days of a campaign?) is valuable signal.

### 10. The Watchdog Has a Silent Ollama Fallback Warning

**The gap:** In `chefflow-watchdog.ps1`, the Ollama start failure path logs: `"ChefFlow will use Gemini fallback until Ollama is ready."` — but the Private AI rule (HARD, NO EXCEPTIONS) says Gemini is never a fallback. This log message is misleading and could create false confidence.

**What to fix:** Update the watchdog log message to: `"Ollama not available — AI features requiring local processing will be disabled until Ollama restarts."` No mention of Gemini. This keeps the log accurate and the privacy guarantee legible.
