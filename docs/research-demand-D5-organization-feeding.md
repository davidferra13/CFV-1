# Research: Feed My Organization (D5)

Date: 2026-03-14
Source: ChefFlow demand-side research
Status: Complete

> Market research conducted 2026-03-14 across Trustpilot, BBB, G2, Quora, industry surveys, trade publications, market research reports, and vendor comparison sites.

---

## Executive Summary

- The US catering market is valued at $55-73 billion (2024), with the corporate/office segment representing 30-39% of total demand and growing at 8.8% CAGR. 43% of companies now offer recurring meal programs, a 17% increase from 2024.
- Office administrators spend 10+ hours per week managing food orders, with dietary restrictions, last-minute changes, and invoicing cited as the top three pain points. The median employee would sacrifice $4,500 in annual salary before giving up workplace food benefits.
- The market is dominated by platforms (ezCater, Fooda, ZeroCater, DoorDash for Business) that aggregate restaurants but struggle with consistency, personalization, and quality control. Private chefs remain largely invisible to corporate buyers despite offering exactly what high-value segments (retreats, executive dining, film sets) need.
- Film/TV craft services is a specialized, high-value niche ($3,000-$4,000/day budgets for decent productions) with no dominant platform, heavy word-of-mouth hiring, and acute pain around schedule unpredictability and dietary accommodations.
- The biggest unmet need across all sub-segments is a single platform that combines reliable chef discovery, dietary intelligence, transparent pricing, and seamless rebooking for recurring needs.

## Sub-Segment Profiles

### A. Office/Corporate Meal Programs

**Who they are:** Office managers, executive assistants, HR/people ops teams, and facilities managers at companies ranging from 20-person startups to Fortune 500 campuses. They order food for daily lunch programs, weekly team meals, client meetings, board dinners, and all-hands events.

**Market size:** The corporate segment holds 30-39% of the US catering market (Grand View Research, MarkNTel Advisors). That translates to roughly $16-28 billion annually depending on the total market estimate used. The segment is growing at 8.8% CAGR, faster than the overall market (Grand View Research). 60% of workplaces plan to spend more on workplace food in 2026 (ezCater Feeding the Workplace Report).

**Frequency:** Daily to weekly for recurring programs; ad-hoc for meetings and events. The 2024 Feeding the Workplace report shows a 32% year-over-year increase in daily and weekly employee meal programs.

### B. Film/TV Production Sets

**Who they are:** Line producers, production managers, production coordinators, and unit production managers (UPMs) responsible for feeding crews of 30-200+ people across 12-16 hour shoot days. Two distinct roles exist: craft services (continuous snacks, beverages, coffee throughout the day) and catering (hot, sit-down meals served twice daily).

**Market size:** No standalone market figure exists, but the US film/TV production industry spent $28.8 billion in 2023 (MPAA). Food typically represents 3-5% of production budgets. For a mid-budget production spending $10M, that is $300K-$500K on food alone.

**Cost range:** Basic craft services runs $10-$22 per person per day. Full-service catering with a dedicated attendant starts at $400/day plus $12-$22 per person per meal (Raindance, Beverly Boy Productions). High-end productions budget $3,000-$4,000/day for food (IndieTalk forums). A 30-person crew with basic packages runs approximately $760/day (Beverly Boy Productions).

### C. Corporate Retreats and Off-Sites

**Who they are:** HR leaders, executive assistants, chief of staff roles, and increasingly dedicated "retreat planners" or agencies. They plan multi-day (typically 2-5 day) programs for teams of 15-200 at venues ranging from Airbnb rentals to dedicated retreat centers.

**Key challenge:** Retreats happen at non-traditional venues (cabins, rented estates, co-working retreats) that often lack commercial kitchen infrastructure. Caterers must bring equipment, manage storage, ensure water availability, and maintain temperature control during transport (The Offsite Co., Culinary Eye).

**Cost range:** $50-$200 per person per day for private chef experiences (GigSalad, Food Fire + Knives). Multi-day retreats with breakfast, lunch, dinner, and snacks for 40+ people can run $150-$300 per person per day all-in.

### D. Conferences and Conventions

**Who they are:** Event planners, association executives, conference organizers, and venue-contracted catering managers. They feed hundreds to thousands of attendees across multi-day events with complex schedules (keynote lunches, breakout sessions, networking receptions, gala dinners).

**Cost range:** Coffee breaks run $5-$15 per person; lunches $20-$50 per person; dinners/galas $50-$150 per person; all-day conference packages $160-$300 per person including beverages (Social Tables, Best Food Trucks, Thumbtack). Beverages add $10-$30 per person (Bark).

**Key constraint:** Most conferences are locked into venue-exclusive catering contracts. The conference organizer often has zero choice in caterer. This sub-segment is largely inaccessible to independent chefs and platforms.

### E. Schools, Camps, and Youth Programs

**Who they are:** School administrators, camp directors, after-school program coordinators, and the food service management companies (FSMCs) that contract with them. Specialized providers include School Caterers, Cater Tots, CampFood.org, and Lunchbox Caterers.

**Key dynamics:** Heavy regulation (USDA nutrition standards, state health codes), tight budgets ($3-$6 per meal in many school districts), and outsourcing trends where schools reduce labor costs by contracting meal preparation to FSMCs or off-site commissaries (Kappan Online). This segment is highly institutional and largely impenetrable for individual chefs or small platforms.

### F. Hospitals and Care Facilities

**Who they are:** Hospital food service directors, dietary managers, and the large FSMCs (Compass Group/Morrison Healthcare, Sodexo, Aramark) that manage 98% of hospital food operations either in-house or outsourced (Community Hospital Corporation).

**Key dynamics:** Patient satisfaction with hospital food averages "okay" (53.3% of respondents), with 35% average plate waste (Barriers and Challenges of an Outsource Hospital Foodservice Operation, UPM 2022). Healthcare foodservice outsourcing saves around 11% on costs. This segment is dominated by massive institutional contractors and is not accessible to independent chefs or small platforms.

## 1. The Trigger

What makes someone enter the "Feed My Organization" need-state?

**Office/Corporate:**

- Return-to-office mandates creating demand for food-as-culture. 88% of business leaders say meals encourage more employees to work on-site (ezCater). Major US cities now hit 65-75% of pre-pandemic occupancy, up from 50% in 2023.
- A new office manager or EA inherits the "food person" role and needs to figure it out fast.
- Company growth crosses a threshold (typically 20-30 employees) where ad-hoc lunch runs stop working.
- Leadership decides food is a retention/culture tool. The median employee would sacrifice $4,500 in annual salary before giving up food benefits (ezCater survey).
- Hybrid "anchor days" (Tuesday/Wednesday/Thursday) create concentrated demand. Companies provide food on in-office days to incentivize attendance.

**Film/TV Production:**

- A production is greenlit and the line producer needs to staff craft services and catering within the production timeline.
- Union requirements (SAG-AFTRA, IATSE) mandate specific meal timing, breaks, and quality standards. Missing a meal penalty triggers overtime costs that dwarf the cost of food.
- A previous craft services provider delivered poorly and the production coordinator is searching for a replacement mid-shoot.

**Corporate Retreats:**

- A company schedules an off-site and realizes the venue has no food infrastructure.
- An executive assistant is asked to "handle food for the retreat" with minimal guidance and a vague budget.
- A previous retreat had food complaints that overshadowed the team-building objectives.

**Conferences:**

- Annual conference planning cycle begins 6-12 months out and food/beverage is one of the largest line items (often 30-40% of total event budget).
- A conference outgrows its previous venue and needs to find new catering at scale.

## 2. The Search Journey

Step by step, what do corporate food buyers actually do?

### Office/Corporate (Most Common Journey)

1. **Google search:** "corporate catering near me," "office lunch delivery," "best catering for office meetings." This surfaces ezCater, local caterers, and delivery platforms.
2. **Ask peers:** Office managers ask other office managers in their building, network, or online communities (Facebook groups, Slack communities, LinkedIn).
3. **Try a platform:** Sign up for ezCater, CaterCow, or ZeroCater. Place a first order. Evaluate food quality, delivery reliability, and ease of reordering.
4. **Build a roster:** After 3-5 orders, the admin develops a shortlist of 3-4 reliable caterers they rotate through. This becomes "the list."
5. **Lock in:** If the company reaches daily/weekly cadence, they either sign a contract with a single provider (ZeroCater, Fooda) or continue self-managing through a platform (ezCater, DoorDash for Business).

### Film/TV Production

1. **Word of mouth:** The production coordinator asks other coordinators, line producers, or unions for referrals. This is the dominant channel. There is no "ezCater for film sets."
2. **Crewing platforms:** Sites like StaffMeUp, Mandy, ProductionHub list craft services providers, but the listings are sparse and quality is unverified.
3. **Local restaurant partnerships:** For smaller productions, coordinators call local restaurants directly for catering drops.
4. **Social media:** Instagram and Facebook groups for local film communities are used to find and vet providers.

### Corporate Retreats

1. **Venue recommendation:** The retreat venue suggests their preferred caterers. This is the most common path.
2. **Google search:** "private chef for corporate retreat [city]," "catering for team off-site."
3. **Platforms:** GigSalad, Thumbtack, Take a Chef for private chef experiences. These are consumer-facing and often lack corporate billing features.
4. **Agency:** Larger companies use retreat planning agencies (The Offsite Co., Moniker Partners) that handle food as part of a complete package.

## 3. Where It Breaks

Platform by platform, what fails for organizational food buyers?

### ezCater

**What it does well:** Massive restaurant network (82,000+), nationwide coverage, self-service ordering, corporate billing, $1.6B valuation proves market demand.

**Where it breaks:**

- **Quality inconsistency.** ezCater is a marketplace, not a quality filter. The food depends entirely on which restaurant partner fulfills the order. "Food delivered cold," "wrong items," "missing dishes" are recurring Trustpilot/BBB complaints.
- **Delivery reliability.** Multiple BBB complaints about orders not delivered at all, or delivered to wrong addresses. One customer was "charged for an order but told at pickup it wasn't paid" (BBB).
- **Customer service loops.** "Spoke to four different people and was disconnected twice" (BBB). Chat representatives "respond from a script instead" of addressing issues (G2).
- **Tips mishandled.** Reports of "stealing tips from drivers" (BBB).
- **No chef relationship.** The food orderer has zero relationship with whoever cooks the food. No personalization, no institutional memory of preferences.

### Fooda

**What it does well:** Innovative pop-up model (restaurants set up on-site), variety through daily rotation, 10M+ meals per year.

**Where it breaks:**

- **Vendors sell out early.** "Vendors pack up before their stated departure time due to selling out" (App Store reviews). Employees who eat late get nothing.
- **Last-minute cancellations.** "Cancellations are announced with only 15-30 minutes notice" (App Store reviews).
- **App reliability.** "App crashes when users try to save required options for a dish" (App Store reviews). Verification emails sent to wrong destination.
- **Restaurant partner economics.** Fooda charges restaurants $100-$300 to serve food but doesn't reimburse when turnout is low (TrueCostOfFooda blog). This creates adversarial vendor relationships.

### ZeroCater

**What it does well:** Curated quality, dedicated account managers, full-service setup with chafing dishes and labels, micro-kitchen/snack management, 450+ kitchen network.

**Where it breaks:**

- **Billing issues.** "Sent an unpaid invoice email after 6+ months despite having a credit card on file" (Trustpilot).
- **Dietary gaps.** "Employees had dietary issues that left them out from getting some of the meals" (G2).
- **Sell-out problem.** "Popular meals would sell out before all employees could get them" (G2).
- **Delivery failures.** "Order sent to wrong address, arrived an hour late, and lacked plates and utensils" (Trustpilot).

### DoorDash for Business

**What it does well:** Massive restaurant network (inherited from consumer platform), individual meal credits (employees choose their own food), spend management dashboard, DashPass integration ($0 delivery fees), automated expensing.

**Where it breaks:**

- **Not built for group orders.** Individual meal credits work for daily lunch perks, but fail for team meetings, client lunches, or events where you need coordinated delivery of one large order.
- **No quality curation.** Same problem as consumer DoorDash: inconsistent quality, cold food, slow delivery.
- **Restaurant fatigue.** Employees quickly exhaust the nearby restaurant options and the "perk" feeling fades.
- **No chef relationship.** Zero personalization or institutional knowledge.

### Private Chef Platforms (GigSalad, Thumbtack, Food Fire + Knives)

**What they do well:** Connect organizations with actual chefs who cook on-site with personalized menus.

**Where they break:**

- **Consumer-oriented.** Built for dinner parties, not corporate procurement. No corporate billing, no recurring scheduling, no dietary management at scale.
- **No vetting transparency.** Reviews exist but credentials, certifications, and insurance verification are inconsistent.
- **Pricing opacity.** "Contact for quote" is the norm. Corporate buyers need budgets before they can get approval.
- **No rebooking workflow.** Each booking is a one-off transaction. No account management, no institutional memory.

## 4. The Decision Factors

What matters most when choosing a food provider for an organization? Ranked by evidence volume:

1. **Reliability and punctuality.** The #1 factor across every sub-segment. "Reliability now tops the list of decision drivers" (Fooda 2025 Trends Report). Late food derails meetings, violates union contracts, and erodes trust in the admin who ordered it.

2. **Dietary accommodation.** 50% of office admins cite managing dietary restrictions as a top frustration (Smart Craving). With allergies, religious requirements, and lifestyle diets, every order is a puzzle. One mistake can be a health emergency or a PR disaster.

3. **Variety and menu fatigue prevention.** "Not enough variety" is the most frequently heard complaint in ongoing programs (ezCater). For daily/weekly programs, rotation is essential; the same Thai place every Tuesday kills enthusiasm within a month.

4. **Ease of ordering and reordering.** Admins managing food alongside 50 other responsibilities need one-click reorders, saved preferences, and minimal friction. "The workload of the harried office assistant increases for every catered meal" (Robin).

5. **Budget control and transparent pricing.** 65% of office admins find payment and invoicing processes stressful (Smart Craving). Corporate buyers need upfront per-person pricing, not "contact for quote." Finance teams need consolidated invoices, not 47 separate restaurant receipts.

6. **Food quality and temperature.** "Even the best food can leave a bad impression if it's served too late, too early, or not at the right temperature" (Tabla Cuisine). Temperature control during delivery is a persistent failure point.

7. **Waste minimization.** Ordering the right amount is the #1 challenge for 33% of food orderers (ezCater). Over-ordering wastes money; under-ordering creates resentment.

## 5. The Constraints That Matter Most

Which constraint dimensions have the biggest impact on organizational food purchasing?

### Headcount Uncertainty

The single biggest operational constraint. Meetings get rescheduled, employees work from home unexpectedly, guests cancel. Ordering for 30 when 18 show up wastes hundreds of dollars. Ordering for 30 when 42 show up creates a crisis. Hybrid work makes headcount prediction nearly impossible on any given day.

### Dietary Complexity at Scale

One person's nut allergy is manageable. When you have 50 employees across vegan, kosher, gluten-free, halal, keto, dairy-free, and multiple allergies, every order becomes a matrix. The consequences of failure range from "someone goes hungry" to "someone goes to the hospital." This is the constraint that most platforms handle poorly because restaurant-based catering operates on fixed menus, not personalized dietary intelligence.

### Procurement and Approval Workflows

B2B food purchasing typically involves 6-10 decision-makers across procurement, finance, operations, and management (Spendflo). Large companies require vendor onboarding (W-9, insurance verification, food safety certifications), purchase orders, net-30 payment terms, and consolidated monthly invoicing. Most catering platforms are built for credit card transactions, not enterprise procurement.

### Schedule Rigidity (Film/Production)

Union rules (SAG-AFTRA Meal Penalty: $25/half hour after 6 hours of continuous work without a meal) make food timing non-negotiable. A late lunch on a 150-person set can cost $3,750 per half hour in meal penalties alone. The food team must adapt instantly when a shoot "that was supposed to wrap by noon might run until midnight" (HowStuffWorks).

### Venue Infrastructure (Retreats)

Many retreat venues lack commercial kitchens, adequate refrigeration, running water access, or proper ventilation. A chef who can cook brilliantly in a commercial kitchen may be completely unprepared for a cabin with a residential stove and a mini-fridge. This constraint filters out most traditional caterers and favors experienced private chefs who are accustomed to working in clients' homes.

## 6. The Trust Barrier

What are organizational food buyers afraid of? What would make them trust a new platform?

### What They Fear

- **Food safety incident.** An allergic reaction, food poisoning outbreak, or contamination event at a corporate lunch or production set is a potential lawsuit, PR crisis, and career-ending event for whoever ordered the food.
- **No-show caterer.** The meeting starts in 30 minutes, the food isn't here, and 40 people are waiting. The admin looks incompetent. This is the nightmare scenario that keeps office managers on "the safe list" of known caterers.
- **Budget overrun.** Hidden fees, service charges, delivery surcharges, and automatic gratuities that push the final bill 30-40% above the quoted price. Finance pushes back; the admin gets blamed.
- **Quality inconsistency.** The first order was great; the third was terrible. Marketplace platforms can't guarantee consistency because different kitchens fulfill different orders.

### What Builds Trust

- **Guaranteed reliability with financial backing.** ezCater's "Relish Guarantee" (order arrives on time and correct, or your money back) is the gold standard. Any new platform needs a similar guarantee.
- **Transparent, all-in pricing.** Show the total cost per person upfront, including delivery, setup, service, and tax. No surprises.
- **Verified chef credentials.** Insurance, food handler certifications, allergen training, references, and review history all visible before booking.
- **Dietary intelligence.** A system that remembers the organization's dietary profile and automatically applies it to every order, so the admin doesn't have to specify "2 vegan, 1 gluten-free, 1 kosher" every single time.
- **Trial order with low commitment.** Let the buyer do one small order (team meeting for 10) before committing to a weekly program for 200.

How do organizational food buyers think about money?

### Per-Person Budgets by Segment

| Segment                 | Low End           | Mid Range           | High End            |
| ----------------------- | ----------------- | ------------------- | ------------------- |
| Office daily lunch      | $12-15/person     | $18-25/person       | $30-40/person       |
| Meeting/event catering  | $15-20/person     | $25-40/person       | $50-75/person       |
| Executive/client dinner | $50/person        | $75-100/person      | $150-200/person     |
| Film craft services     | $10-15/person/day | $22-35/person/day   | $50-125/person/day  |
| Film catering (meals)   | $15/person/meal   | $25-40/person/meal  | $60-100/person/meal |
| Retreat (full day)      | $50/person/day    | $100-150/person/day | $200-300/person/day |
| Conference lunch        | $20/person        | $35-50/person       | $75-100/person      |
| Conference gala dinner  | $50/person        | $75-100/person      | $150+/person        |

### How They Think About Value

- **Not the cheapest option.** Corporate buyers are not optimizing for the lowest price. They are optimizing for "no complaints, no incidents, no surprises." Reliability commands a premium.
- **Per-person is the universal unit.** Every buyer, from a startup ordering lunch to a production coordinator feeding 150, thinks in cost-per-person-per-meal.
- **Total cost transparency matters more than low price.** A $22/person quote that arrives at $22/person is preferred over a $18/person quote that becomes $28/person after delivery fees, service charges, and auto-gratuity.
- **Food budgets are growing.** 53% of corporate food orderers plan to increase budgets (ezCater 2024). 60% of workplaces plan to spend more on food in 2026. Nearly a third expect budget increases of 25% or more.
- **The "salary sacrifice" benchmark.** The median employee would give up $4,500/year in salary to keep food benefits (ezCater). This gives employers a clear ROI calculation: $4,500/employee/year in perceived value for roughly $2,000-$3,000/employee/year in actual food spend.

## 9. The Retention Hook

What turns a one-time organizational food order into a repeat relationship?

### The Admin's "Safe List"

The strongest retention mechanism in corporate catering is not a loyalty program. It is the admin's mental (or literal) shortlist of 3-4 providers they trust. Once a caterer earns a spot on this list, they get repeat orders for months or years without competing. Getting onto this list requires: (1) flawless first delivery, (2) good food, (3) easy reordering.

### Specific Retention Drivers

1. **One-click reorder.** Corporate food orderers save 10+ hours per week using platforms with reorder functionality (ezCater). The ability to duplicate a past order with one click is the single biggest retention feature for recurring programs.

2. **Consistent point of contact.** ZeroCater's dedicated account manager model creates "stickiness" because the admin has a person who knows their preferences, their building's loading dock, their CEO's nut allergy, and their budget cycle. Switching means re-educating someone new.

3. **Dietary memory.** A system that remembers "always include 2 vegan options, label all allergens, David is celiac" and applies it automatically to every order. This removes cognitive load from the admin and reduces error risk.

4. **Predictable billing.** Monthly consolidated invoices that match the PO, arrive on time, and don't require chasing. Finance teams that trust the vendor's billing never push to switch.

5. **Proactive variety management.** For daily/weekly programs, menu fatigue is the #1 killer. A provider that proactively rotates menus, suggests seasonal changes, or introduces new options without being asked retains clients longer.

6. **Recovery from failure.** Every provider eventually has a bad delivery. The providers that retain clients are the ones that acknowledge the failure immediately, make it right (refund, credit, replacement), and prevent recurrence. "ezCater works hard to make things right when orders go wrong" (Trustpilot) is the kind of recovery that builds loyalty.

### Retention by Sub-Segment

- **Office programs:** Retention is extremely high once established. Switching costs are real (re-vetting vendors, re-training admins, risking quality dips). The average corporate catering relationship lasts 2-3 years.
- **Film/TV:** Retention is production-based. A great craft services provider gets hired for the next production by the same coordinator. Relationships follow people, not companies.
- **Retreats:** Low natural retention because retreats are infrequent (1-4 per year). Retention depends on the chef's ability to stay top-of-mind between events.
- **Conferences:** Annual retention tied to venue contracts. If the conference changes venue, the caterer often changes too.

## 10. The Unmet Need

What would organizational food buyers use if it existed?

### The Gap in the Market

No platform today combines all of the following:

1. **Chef-first, not restaurant-first.** Existing platforms aggregate restaurants. The unmet need is a platform that aggregates chefs: culinary professionals who can cook on-site, personalize menus, manage dietary complexity, and build relationships with the organization over time.

2. **Dietary intelligence at the organization level.** A persistent dietary profile for the organization that every chef inherits. "This company has 3 vegans, 2 gluten-free, 1 severe nut allergy, and a general preference for lighter lunches." Every order, every chef, every time, without the admin re-specifying it.

3. **Transparent, all-in, per-person pricing.** Show the buyer exactly what they will pay before they commit. No hidden fees. No "service charge" surprises. The quoted price is the final price.

4. **Recurring scheduling with flexibility.** Book a chef for every Tuesday and Thursday, with the ability to cancel individual days (with reasonable notice) without losing the recurring relationship. Like a gym membership for office food.

5. **Corporate procurement compatibility.** Net-30 invoicing, PO numbers, W-9 on file, certificate of insurance, consolidated monthly statements. The features that enterprise buyers require but consumer-oriented chef platforms don't offer.

6. **Cross-context continuity.** The same platform that books a chef for weekly office lunches can also book that chef (or a different one) for the company retreat, the client dinner, and the holiday party. One vendor relationship, one billing relationship, one dietary profile, across all contexts.

### Sub-Segment Specific Unmet Needs

**Office/Corporate:**

- A "chef residency" model where a dedicated chef is assigned to a company for ongoing programs, building institutional knowledge (preferences, dietary needs, kitchen layout, delivery logistics) over time.
- Headcount prediction tools that integrate with HRIS/calendar systems to estimate how many people will actually be in the office on a given day.

**Film/TV Production:**

- A discovery platform for production-experienced chefs and craft services providers, searchable by location, availability, and crew size capacity. Currently this is 100% word-of-mouth.
- Budget templates and production-specific billing (daily rates, meal penalties tracking, union compliance documentation).

**Corporate Retreats:**

- "Venue-aware" chef matching: a chef who knows the specific retreat venue, its kitchen limitations, and has cooked there before. This eliminates the "will the chef be able to handle a cabin kitchen?" anxiety.
- All-inclusive retreat food packages with transparent pricing: breakfast/lunch/dinner/snacks for X people for Y days at $Z per person per day, no surprises.

**Conferences:**

- This sub-segment is largely locked into venue-exclusive catering contracts. The unmet need is for conference organizers who want to break free of overpriced hotel catering but don't have the infrastructure to manage independent caterers.

## ChefFlow Opportunity Analysis

### Where ChefFlow Fits

ChefFlow's strongest opportunity in the D5 need-state is in the **high-value, relationship-driven sub-segments** where platforms fail and private chefs excel:

1. **Corporate retreats and off-sites** (private chef cooking on-site at non-traditional venues)
2. **Executive dining and client entertainment** (high-touch, personalized, premium)
3. **Film/TV production catering** (experienced chefs who can handle demanding schedules)
4. **Small-to-midsize company meal programs** (weekly "chef residency" model, 20-80 employees)

### Where ChefFlow Should NOT Compete

1. **Large-scale conference catering** (venue-locked, institutional, commodity pricing)
2. **Hospital/healthcare food service** (dominated by Compass/Sodexo/Aramark, heavily regulated)
3. **School/camp food service** (USDA regulated, ultra-low budgets, institutional contracts)
4. **Commodity office lunch delivery** (ezCater, DoorDash for Business own this; competing on restaurant volume is a losing game)

### The Private Chef Advantage

What makes a private chef fundamentally better than a restaurant marketplace for the high-value segments:

- **On-site cooking** means food is never cold, never late (it's cooked where it's eaten)
- **Personalized menus** solve the dietary complexity problem at the source
- **Chef relationship** creates institutional memory (preferences, restrictions, kitchen quirks)
- **Flexibility** for non-traditional venues (retreats, homes, production locations)
- **Premium experience** justifies premium pricing that chefs need to sustain their business

### Features ChefFlow Would Need

To serve organizational buyers, ChefFlow would need to add or emphasize:

- Corporate billing (invoicing, PO numbers, net-30 terms)
- Organization-level dietary profiles (persistent across all bookings)
- Recurring scheduling with flexible cancellation
- Multi-meal/multi-day packages (retreat pricing, weekly programs)
- Headcount management tools
- Chef credentials and insurance verification visible to buyers
- Group ordering workflow (one admin books for many people)
