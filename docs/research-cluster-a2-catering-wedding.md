## Archetype 1: Catering Chef

### Who They Are

Catering chefs run event-based food businesses serving weddings, corporate events, galas, and private parties. They range from solo operators running everything from a home kitchen to mid-size operations with 10-30 part-time staff. They handle menu planning, food costing, proposals, logistics, staff scheduling, dietary tracking, invoicing, and client communication, often simultaneously. Many started as restaurant chefs who transitioned to catering for better margins and more creative control.

- U.S. catering market reached approximately $72 billion in 2024, growing at 6.2% CAGR ([Expert Market Research](https://www.expertmarketresearch.com/reports/united-states-catering-market))

### Top Pain Points

1. **Staffing crisis** - 86% understaffed, 50% no-show rate on busy days, 20% annual turnover
2. **Food cost volatility** - prices shifted 5.4% in 2023 alone, caterers sell now but buy later
3. **Proposal/admin time sink** - 8-10 hours per event on proposals, shopping lists, recipe writing
4. **Spreadsheet chaos** - manual tracking of costs, allergens, orders across disconnected files
5. **Software that doesn't fit** - existing tools are expensive, outdated, or built for venues not caterers
6. **Last-minute guest count changes** - cascading impact on food, staff, logistics
7. **Payment collection** - deposits, change orders, late payments, unclear terms
8. **Scaling without losing quality** - 57% of restaurants find scaling catering sales challenging
9. **Dietary restriction tracking** - cross-contamination risk, manual spreadsheet tracking
10. **Burnout** - 80% burnout rate in hospitality, 70% experience physical symptoms

## Archetype 2: Wedding Specialist Chef

### Who They Are

Wedding specialist chefs focus exclusively or primarily on weddings. They handle tastings, high-touch proposals with customized menus, dietary accommodation for 100-300+ guests, vendor coordination with planners and venues, and emotionally-charged client relationships. They operate in a high-stakes environment where one bad event can destroy their reputation via WeddingWire/The Knot reviews. Their sales cycle is long (6-18 months from inquiry to event), seasonal (peak May-October), and involves multiple tastings and revisions.

### Market Size Signals

- Wedding services market valued at $64.93 billion in 2024 ([Grand View Research](https://www.grandviewresearch.com/industry-analysis/us-wedding-services-market-report))

### Top Pain Points

1. **Proposal time sink** - custom menus, costing, revisions for every couple
2. **Tasting logistics** - scheduling, food cost for tastings, managing expectations
3. **Review reputation management** - one bad review on WeddingWire/The Knot is devastating
4. **Seasonal burnout** - wedding season (May-Oct) is relentless, then drought in winter
5. **Dietary complexity** - managing 10+ dietary restrictions per event across 200+ guests
6. **Vendor coordination** - working with planners, venues, florists, each with their own process
7. **Deposit/payment disputes** - non-refundable deposit confusion, late final payments
8. **Emotional clients** - highest-stakes day of someone's life, zero margin for error
9. **Venue restrictions** - forced to use venue's preferred vendors/equipment
10. **Last-minute changes** - guest count shifts, menu swaps close to the date

## Cross-Archetype Analysis

### Shared Pain Points (Both Archetypes)

1. **Admin overwhelm** - proposals, costing, invoicing eat creative time
2. **Spreadsheet dependence** - most start with Excel/Sheets, outgrow them, but alternatives are expensive or clunky
3. **Staff unreliability** - no-shows, scheduling chaos with part-time workers
4. **Food cost volatility** - sell now, buy later, prices shift between proposal and event
5. **Client communication** - emails lost, slow responses, no single source of truth
6. **Burnout** - 80% industry burnout rate, seasonal intensity
7. **Software frustration** - existing tools are either too expensive, too complex, too outdated, or not built for caterers

### Key Differentiators

| Factor              | Catering Chef                         | Wedding Specialist                           |
| ------------------- | ------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------- |
| Event frequency     | Higher volume, shorter cycles         | Lower volume, longer planning cycles         |
| Client emotion      | Moderate (corporate is transactional) | Extreme (most important day of their life)   |
| Review sensitivity  | Important                             | Career-defining                              |
| Tasting frequency   | Occasional                            | Every client                                 |
| Menu customization  | Template-based with variations        | Fully bespoke per couple                     |
| Vendor coordination | Moderate                              | Intense (planner, venue, florist, DJ, etc.)  |
| Seasonality impact  | Year-round with peaks                 | Extreme May-October crush                    |
| Proposal complexity | Moderate                              | High (photos, narrative, multiple revisions) |

### Where ChefFlow Wins

Based on this research, the strongest competitive positioning for ChefFlow against these archetypes:

1. **Mobile-first** - most competitors lack good mobile apps; caterers work on-site, not at desks
2. **Affordable** - Caterease is $3,500, Curate is $3,400/yr, Tripleseat is expensive; there's a gap at the $50-100/mo range for solo/small operators
3. **All-in-one without the bloat** - caterers want simple tools, not enterprise software
4. **No per-inquiry pricing** - Curate's model punishes busy caterers
5. **AI for admin, not for cooking** - automate proposals, costing, follow-ups; never touch recipes
6. **Dietary tracking built in** - spreadsheet allergen tracking is a safety hazard
7. **Real-time food costing** - prices change; static spreadsheets lie about margins
8. **Email that actually delivers** - Tripleseat and Better Cater both have email deliverability problems
