## 1. Business Owner

**Core question:** How do restaurant owners manage their presence on Yelp, Google, TripAdvisor? What's their relationship with being listed on platforms?

### Current Workflow (Step by Step)

1. **Claim listings manually on each platform** - Google Business Profile, Yelp, TripAdvisor, Apple Maps, Facebook, Bing, Foursquare, and 80+ other directories. Each has a different claim process, different verification methods (phone, email, postcard), and different dashboards.
2. **Maintain NAP consistency** - Name, Address, Phone must match exactly across all directories. A single typo on one platform can fragment search presence.
3. **Update menus, hours, photos, and descriptions** - Every platform needs current info. Seasonal menus, holiday hours, new photos. Each requires a separate login and manual update.
4. **Monitor and respond to reviews** - Yelp, Google, TripAdvisor, OpenTable all have separate review streams. Owners must check each individually or use aggregation tools.
5. **Track performance** - Each platform has its own analytics dashboard. No unified view of which directory drives the most traffic.

### Where Things Break

- **Platform sprawl is unmanageable.** 56% of consumers say they would not consider a business with incorrect information in online directories ([TNW](https://thenextweb.com/news/inaccurate-business-listings-are-costing-companies)). Yet managing consistency across 90+ directories is described as "a full-time job that nobody has time for."
- **Yelp auto-unclaims inactive pages.** If an owner hasn't logged in for 90 days, Yelp may show the page as unclaimed, making it look abandoned ([Yelp Claiming FAQ](https://docs.developer.yelp.com/docs/claiming-faq)).
- **Verification is inconsistent.** Yelp typically only offers phone verification. Some owners wait days or weeks. No two platforms use the same process ([BrightLocal](https://www.brightlocal.com/learn/how-to-add-or-claim-a-yelp-business-listing/)).
- **Multi-location is exponentially harder.** A 5-location restaurant group has 450+ individual listing pages to manage across directories ([Malou](https://www.malou.io/en-us/blog/online-marketing-for-restaurants-2025)).
- **Small operators avoid the problem entirely.** Small to medium-sized restaurant owners "sometimes hesitate to fully embrace online strategies" because technology feels intimidating or the setup cost seems high ([BU Hospitality Review](https://www.bu.edu/bhr/2019/03/20/how-can-single-unit-restaurants-strive-for-powerful-online-presence/)).

### Workarounds Currently Used

- **Listing management platforms:** Yext ($199-$499/year per location), Moz Local ($16/month per location), BrightLocal, Chatmeter. These sync NAP data from one dashboard to multiple directories via API or data aggregators ([Guideflow](https://www.guideflow.com/blog/local-listing-management-software-tools)).
- **Ignoring most platforms.** Many small operators only manage Google and Yelp and let everything else go stale.
- **Hiring a marketing person or agency.** Multi-location groups outsource listing management entirely, but this costs $2,000-$5,000/month.

### What's Missing

- **No directory puts the operator in control by default.** Operators are always reacting to platforms that listed them without permission. The relationship starts adversarial.
- **No cross-directory accuracy audit tool that's free.** Operators don't even know where they're listed incorrectly until a customer complains.
- **No lightweight claim mechanism.** Every platform forces full account creation before letting an operator correct their own data.

## 2. Employer

**Core question:** How do food industry employers find talent, assess competitors, and position themselves in the market?

### Current Workflow (Step by Step)

1. **Use job boards** - Indeed, Culinary Agents, Poached Jobs, Craigslist for line-level hiring. LinkedIn for management.
2. **Hire specialized recruiters for executive roles** - Food and beverage recruiters (Kinsa Group, Bristol Associates, SCM Talent Group) charge 15-25% of first-year salary. They provide competitive intelligence as a side benefit: compensation trends, which restaurants are closing/restructuring, chef movements ([Kinsa](https://kinsa.com/), [SCM Talent](https://scmtalent.com/food-and-beverage-recruiters/)).
3. **Manual competitive monitoring** - Visit competitor restaurants, read their reviews, check their menus and pricing, observe their staff levels. This is done in person, not digitally.
4. **Attend industry events** - NRA Show, local restaurant associations, chef competitions for networking and talent scouting.
5. **Track turnover informally** - Watch for "now hiring" signs at competitors, monitor social media for staff departures.

### Where Things Break

- **Over 70% of US food manufacturers reported difficulty finding qualified workers** as of 2023. The talent crisis is severe at every level ([Adecco](https://www.adecco.com/employers/industries/food-industry)).
- **Competitive intelligence is informal and fragmented.** There's no structured way for a restaurant group to see all operators in their market, their size, their cuisine focus, or their staffing patterns without paying $10K+ for a consultant.
- **Recruiter intelligence is paywalled.** The market insight recruiters provide (who's closing, who's restructuring, where talent is available) is only accessible if you're paying for an executive search.
- **No real-time competitor monitoring tool.** Restaurant owners learn a competitor closed when they drive past and see the sign down.

### Workarounds Currently Used

- **Google Alerts** for competitor names. Low signal, high noise.
- **Yelp/Google Maps browsing** to count competitors in an area. Extremely manual.
- **Word of mouth** through industry networks. This is the #1 source of competitive intelligence for most operators.
- **Paying for recruiter market reports** when making expansion decisions.

### What's Missing

- **A live map of the competitive landscape** that doesn't require manual research.
- **Structured data on operator density by cuisine, geography, and business type.** This data exists in scattered directories but nobody aggregates it for business use.
- **Staff movement and closure tracking.** Knowing that 3 Italian restaurants in your area closed in 6 months is valuable market intelligence that currently only surfaces through gossip.

## 3. Corporate / Enterprise

**Core question:** How do corporate event planners, HR teams, and office managers find catering and food services?

### Current Workflow (Step by Step)

1. **Identify the need** - Meeting catering, office lunch program, corporate event, employee appreciation. Different needs go through different channels.
2. **Search for vendors** - Google searches, ask colleagues, check ezCater (75,000+ restaurants, nationwide) or CaterCow (curated, vetted vendors). Some companies use Fooda for rotating restaurant popups ([ezCater](https://www.ezcater.com/), [CaterCow](https://www.catercow.com/)).
3. **For ongoing/large programs, write an RFP** - Formal 8-week process for corporate cafeteria or large food service contracts. Distribute to potential vendors, collect proposals, score with standardized criteria, conduct food tastings, check references, negotiate terms ([Fooda](https://www.fooda.com/blog/corporate-cafeteria-rfp-guide-and-template)).
4. **For ad-hoc catering, browse and order** - Platforms like ezCater let you filter by location, cuisine, dietary needs, budget, and order directly. Reviews and ratings guide selection.
5. **Evaluate on cost, quality, reliability** - Corporate teams prioritize: on-time delivery, dietary accommodation, budget compliance, presentation quality.

### Where Things Break

- **Pricing models vary dramatically.** Per-head, per-platter, minimum order, delivery fees, service charges. Comparing vendors is extremely difficult without standardized pricing ([Fooda](https://www.fooda.com/blog/corporate-cafeteria-rfp-guide-and-template)).
- **Discovery is limited to platforms.** If a local caterer isn't on ezCater, they don't exist to corporate buyers. The platform becomes the gatekeeper.
- **Quality is hard to verify at scale.** CaterCow's value proposition is that they vet vendors through a rigorous process, implying that the default state (ezCater, Google) does not adequately filter for quality.
- **Dietary and compliance requirements are complex.** Corporate events need allergen handling, kosher/halal options, sustainability certifications. This information is rarely standardized across vendor listings.
- **Small and independent caterers are invisible.** Enterprise platforms favor large operations that can handle volume. A talented private chef or small catering operation won't appear on ezCater.

### Workarounds Currently Used

- **Maintaining internal "approved vendor" lists** that get outdated fast.
- **Using 2-3 platforms simultaneously** (ezCater for volume, local searches for special events, referrals for high-end).
- **Hiring food service consultants** for large contract decisions ($5K-$25K for a vendor selection project).
- **Relying on a single admin's personal knowledge** of local food options, creating a single point of failure.

### What's Missing

- **A comprehensive directory of food operators filterable by capability, not just cuisine.** Corporate buyers need to filter by: minimum order size, dietary certifications, delivery radius, event capacity, insurance/licensing status.
- **Standardized capability profiles.** Does this caterer do 500-person events? Do they have ServSafe certification? Can they handle nut-free facilities? This data isn't aggregated anywhere.
- **A way to discover operators who aren't on the major platforms.** The best caterer for a corporate event might be a private chef who never signed up for ezCater.

## 4. Entrepreneur

**Core question:** How do aspiring restaurant owners research locations, competition, and market gaps before opening?

### Current Workflow (Step by Step)

1. **Define concept and target market** - Decide on cuisine type, price point, service style. This determines what competitive research to do.
2. **Analyze demographics** - Pull census data, household income, population density, age distribution for target neighborhoods. Free tools: Census.gov, city planning data. Paid: Esri, Placer.ai ([Restaurant365](https://www.restaurant365.com/blog/how-to-conduct-a-restaurant-competitive-analysis-in-9-simple-steps/)).
3. **Map competitors manually** - Google Maps, Yelp, walk/drive the area. Identify 10-12 direct and indirect competitors. For foot traffic concepts, search within 1 mile. For drive-to concepts, search within 10 miles ([Restaurant365](https://www.restaurant365.com/blog/how-to-conduct-a-restaurant-competitive-analysis-in-9-simple-steps/)).
4. **Visit competitors in person** - Eat at competitor restaurants, observe customer flow at different times/days, talk to diners, assess staff efficiency and atmosphere ([Toast](https://pos.toasttab.com/blog/on-the-line/restaurant-market-research)).
5. **Analyze menus and pricing** - Study best sellers, portion sizes, ingredient use, customization options, pricing strategies. This is done by physically collecting menus or browsing websites.
6. **Read reviews systematically** - Yelp and OpenTable reviews of competitors reveal service gaps, customer pain points, and unmet demand.
7. **Identify market gaps** - Look for underserved cuisines, price points, dining occasions, or neighborhoods. 42% of startup failures occur because there was no market need ([FasterCapital](https://fastercapital.com/articles/competitive-market-analysis-essential-for-startup-success-in-2025--254317.html)).
8. **Build financial projections** - Use competitor data to estimate revenue potential. Industry reports (IBISWorld, NRA) provide benchmarks.

### Where Things Break

- **Location intelligence is expensive.** Placer.ai and SiteZeus provide foot traffic data and trade area analysis, but pricing starts at thousands per year. The global location intelligence market is $21 billion and growing at 16.8% CAGR ([Placer.ai](https://www.placer.ai/solutions/restaurants), [SiteZeus](https://sitezeus.com/solutions/industry/restaurant/)).
- **Competitor mapping is brutally manual.** There's no tool that shows "all Italian restaurants within 5 miles of this address with their price point, years in business, and review scores" in one view.
- **Gap analysis is guesswork.** Entrepreneurs can identify what exists in an area but have no structured way to see what's missing. "There are 12 pizza places and zero Thai restaurants" requires manually categorizing every operator.
- **Data is scattered across platforms.** Google has hours and reviews. Yelp has different reviews and price ranges. The local health department has inspection scores. The SBA has industry benchmarks. None of this is connected.
- **Market research reports are expensive.** IBISWorld and Mintel reports cost $1,000-$5,000 each. Plunkett's restaurant industry almanac costs hundreds.

### Workarounds Currently Used

- **Google Maps as the primary research tool.** Search by cuisine, zoom to the area, count pins. This is the most common approach.
- **Excel spreadsheets tracking competitors.** Manually built databases of competitors with name, cuisine, price point, review score, hours.
- **SBA and SCORE free consulting.** Government programs provide basic market analysis guidance but not data.
- **Franchise feasibility studies.** Franchisors (like Jimmy's Egg) provide market analysis for their concept, but only if you're buying their franchise ([Jimmy's Egg Franchise](https://jimmyseggfranchise.com/the-ultimate-checklist-for-aspiring-restaurant-franchise-owners/)).
- **BizMiner reports.** Industry financial benchmarks for 5,000+ lines of business, available at the zip code level. Paid but more affordable than IBISWorld.

### What's Missing

- **An instant competitive landscape snapshot for any address.** "Show me every food business within 3 miles, categorized by type and price point." This doesn't exist as a free or affordable tool.
- **Market saturation analysis.** "This zip code has 4x the national average of pizza restaurants" is a data point that should be trivially accessible but isn't.
- **Historical data on closures.** Knowing that the last 3 restaurants at a specific address all closed within a year is critical intelligence that's nearly impossible to find.
- **A free alternative to Placer.ai for small entrepreneurs.** Foot traffic data and trade area analysis are locked behind enterprise pricing.

## 5. Manager

**Core question:** How do food service managers handle vendor relationships, menu sourcing, competitive pricing?

### Current Workflow (Step by Step)

1. **Maintain a vendor roster** - Most managers work with 5-15 suppliers: broadline distributors (Sysco, US Foods), specialty distributors, local farms, beverage distributors. Each requires separate ordering, invoicing, and relationship management ([SynergySuite](https://www.synergysuite.com/blog/where-do-restaurants-get-their-food/)).
2. **Negotiate pricing quarterly or annually** - Set contracts with volume commitments for core ingredients. Renegotiate when costs fluctuate.
3. **Scout new vendors** - When quality drops, prices rise, or new menu items need new ingredients. Discovery happens through: distributor sales reps, industry trade shows, word of mouth, Google searches, digital procurement platforms ([RestaurantWare](https://www.restaurantware.com/blogs/kitchen-and-cooking-tips/sourcing_local-_benefits_and_challenges_for_foodservice)).
4. **Monitor competitor pricing** - Visit competitors, check their menus online, read review sites for perceived value signals. Revenue Management Solutions (RMS) offers a Restaurant Price Index benchmarking 235,000 locations, but it's an enterprise tool ([RMS](https://www.revenuemanage.com/competitor-price-intelligence/)).
5. **Track KPIs for existing vendors** - On-time delivery, product quality, pricing accuracy, responsiveness. Most managers do this informally or in spreadsheets ([SynergySuite](https://www.synergysuite.com/blog/restaurant-vendor-management/)).
6. **Diversify supply sources** - Build backup vendor lists for business continuity. The supply chain disruptions of 2020-2023 taught managers never to rely on a single source.

### Where Things Break

- **Vendor discovery is relationship-dependent.** Finding new, especially local, suppliers relies on networking rather than any structured database. "Coordinating deliveries with multiple local suppliers can complicate logistics, demanding more time and resources" ([RestaurantWare](https://www.restaurantware.com/blogs/kitchen-and-cooking-tips/sourcing_local-_benefits_and_challenges_for_foodservice)).
- **Competitive pricing data is siloed.** RMS and Toast benchmarking provide pricing intelligence, but only for their customers. An independent operator has no affordable way to benchmark their menu prices against the local market.
- **No local vendor marketplace.** If a manager wants to find all specialty cheese producers within 50 miles, there's no single place to look. It's Google searches, farmers market visits, and asking around.
- **Food procurement involves "a large number of suppliers, even for smaller businesses"** and keeping up with changes in pricing and terms is a "very complex task" ([SpendEdge](https://www.spendedge.com/blogs/9-challenges-facing-procurement-food-beverage-industry/)).

### Workarounds Currently Used

- **Sysco/US Foods as the default.** Broadline distributors win by convenience even when prices are higher, because managers don't have time to source from 15 vendors.
- **ERP and procurement platforms** (Oracle, NetSuite, Restroworks) for multi-location chains. These are $50K+ implementations.
- **Informal benchmarking clubs.** Managers in the same market share pricing data over drinks. Completely informal, unscalable.
- **Digital procurement platforms** (BlueCart, Choco, MarketMan) that connect to distributors but don't help with local vendor discovery.

### What's Missing

- **A local food operator directory usable for vendor scouting.** "Who makes fresh pasta within 30 miles?" is a basic question with no good answer.
- **Competitive menu and pricing benchmarking for independents.** RMS tracks 235,000 locations, but the data is behind enterprise pricing. Independent operators are flying blind.
- **A structured way to find specialty suppliers.** Farmers markets, artisan producers, local bakeries, small-batch sauce makers. These operators exist in directories already but aren't surfaced for B2B discovery.

### What the Directory Should Prioritize for Each Persona

#### Entrepreneur: "Market Intelligence in One Click"

The entrepreneur's entire competitive research process (steps 3-7 above) is manual, scattered, and expensive. The directory can collapse it.

**Priority features:**

- **Instant competitive landscape report for any address.** Enter an address, get every food operator within 1/3/5/10 miles, categorized by type, cuisine, price point, years in business.
- **Market saturation analysis.** "This zip code has 2.3x the national average of Mexican restaurants and 0.4x the average of Thai restaurants."
- **Gap identification.** "Underserved categories in this area: Thai, Ethiopian, Vegan, Late-night dining."
- **Historical closure data.** "3 restaurants closed at this address in 4 years" (if available from the data).

**What makes them say "I need this":** "Before ChefFlow, I spent 3 weeks mapping every restaurant in a 5-mile radius on a spreadsheet. Now I get it in 10 seconds."

#### Corporate / Enterprise: "Find Exactly the Right Food Operator"

Corporate buyers need to filter by capability, not just cuisine. The major platforms (ezCater, CaterCow) are limited to operators who opted in. A comprehensive directory changes the game.

**Priority features:**

- **Capability-based filtering.** Event capacity, delivery radius, cuisine type, dietary accommodations, certification status.
- **Operator profiles with business-relevant data.** Not just "Italian restaurant" but: years in operation, location count, service types (dine-in, catering, food truck, private chef).
- **Exportable shortlists.** Corporate procurement teams need to pull lists into RFP documents and comparison spreadsheets.
- **Coverage that includes operators not on major platforms.** Private chefs, small caterers, popup operators. The directory's value is comprehensiveness.

**What makes them say "I need this":** "We needed a halal caterer for 200 people in suburban Connecticut. ezCater had 2 options. ChefFlow's directory showed us 11."

#### Business Owner: "Control Your Own Listing (Finally)"

The directory already has their data. The question is whether they see it as a threat ("who put me here?") or a tool ("I can control how I appear").

**Priority features:**

- **One-click claim with instant verification.** Do not repeat Yelp's painful claim process. Let operators verify via phone or email in under 60 seconds.
- **Accuracy audit.** "Here's how you appear across the internet. Here's what's wrong." Show them the problem, offer the fix.
- **Profile enrichment tools.** Let them add information that matters: dietary capabilities, event capacity, catering availability, certifications. Things Google and Yelp don't capture.
- **No upselling, no hostage-taking.** The moment this looks like Yelp's model (pay us or suffer), operators will revolt. The value proposition must be: "This is your page. You control it. Free."

**What makes them say "I need this":** "For the first time, a directory asked me what I want people to know about my business instead of just scraping my data and selling ads against it."

#### Manager: "Find Vendors and Benchmark Locally"

Managers need the directory to surface operators as potential business partners, not just competitors.

**Priority features:**

- **B2B discovery filters.** "Show me all bakeries within 20 miles that do wholesale." "Show me all farms within 50 miles." Operators tagged by B2B availability.
- **Local competitive benchmarking.** "The average entree price for Italian restaurants in your zip code is $22. You're at $18." (Requires menu price data, which may or may not be in the directory.)
- **Supplier diversity data.** For multi-location chains with supplier diversity requirements: minority-owned, women-owned, veteran-owned food businesses in a region.

**What makes them say "I need this":** "I found a local pasta maker 15 minutes away that I never knew existed. We've been ordering from Sysco for 3 years."

#### Employer: "Competitive Landscape at a Glance"

The employer use case is the weakest standalone value, but it layers well on top of other features.

**Priority features:**

- **Market density visualization.** How many food operators in this market? Growing or shrinking?
- **Competitor profiles.** What are nearby operators doing? How many locations? What cuisine? (This overlaps heavily with the entrepreneur use case.)
- **Closure tracking.** Which operators closed recently? This signals talent availability and market shifts.

**What makes them say "I need this":** "I knew 3 restaurants in my area closed this quarter before anyone else did. I hired their sous chef."

### The Core Reframing: From "Creepy Database" to "Market Intelligence Platform"

The directory currently feels like a surveillance tool because it offers raw data with no context for who benefits and how. The fix is persona-specific framing:

| Current framing                               | Better framing                                                          |
| --------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| "We have data on every restaurant in America" | "See the full competitive landscape for any location"                   |
| "We scraped your business listing"            | "Claim your profile and control how you appear"                         |
| "A database of food operators"                | "Market intelligence for food industry professionals"                   |
| "Search our directory"                        | "Research a market" / "Find the right operator" / "Claim your business" |

**The directory is not a product. It is the data layer beneath 3-4 distinct products:**

1. **Market Intelligence** (for entrepreneurs and employers) - competitive landscape, gap analysis, saturation data
2. **Operator Discovery** (for corporate buyers and managers) - find the right food business for a specific need
3. **Listing Control** (for business owners) - claim, correct, and enrich your profile
4. **Vendor Scout** (for managers) - B2B discovery of food suppliers, producers, and specialty operators

Each of these is a different entry point into the same data, with different UI, different language, and different value propositions.
