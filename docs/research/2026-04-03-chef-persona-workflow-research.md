# Chef Persona Workflow Research

**Date:** 2026-04-03
**Agent:** Research
**Purpose:** Document real workflows, breakpoints, workarounds, and gaps for three food service personas relevant to ChefFlow.

---

## Persona 1: Private / Personal Chef

### Real Daily/Weekly Workflow (Step by Step)

**1. Menu Planning (Evening Before Service)**

- Review client dietary restrictions, allergies, and preferences from notes
- Select dishes based on prior client conversations (often via text/email)
- Input recipes into whatever system they use (software, paper, Google Docs)
- Generate shopping lists organized by store section/location
- Print or prepare: menus with reheating instructions, container labels, recipe copies for reference during cooking

**2. Client Communication and Menu Approval**

- Send menu proposal to client via email, text, or PDF attachment
- Wait for confirmation (deadlines range wildly: 14 days before to morning-of, depending on the chef)
- Handle last-minute dietary changes, guest additions, and preference shifts
- Track each client's communication style preference (some want texts, some want formal emails, some want phone calls)
- Maintain notes on each client: "prefers text updates," "responds best to visual menus," "likes a 5-minute call"

**3. Shopping (Morning of Service)**

- Travel to multiple stores (specialty markets, produce, protein, dry goods)
- Navigate lists across multiple clients if doing back-to-back cook days
- Manage packing logistics (one chef described packing groceries heaviest-to-lightest for taxi transport in Manhattan)
- Handle price fluctuations, out-of-stock items, on-the-fly substitutions

**4. Cooking (Typically 3-6 Hours per Client)**

- Arrive at client's unfamiliar kitchen, adapt to their equipment and layout
- Follow a "battle plan" cooking order: longest-cooking items first
- Manage 3-5 active dishes simultaneously across burners and oven
- Handle distractions (pets, children, client conversation, being watched)
- Prepare 8-9 different dishes (mains and sides) for a weekly client

**5. Cooling, Packaging, and Labeling (~2 Hours)**

- Rapid cool dishes using ice baths, fans, or stirring (food safety: pass through danger zone within 2 hours)
- Package each meal into separate containers
- Label every container (dish name, date, reheating instructions)
- Organize neatly in client's fridge

**6. Cleanup and Departure**

- Clean as you go during cooking; finish with sweeping, mopping, organizing
- Typical departure by 4 PM for a noon start

**7. Administrative Close-Out (Evening)**

- Create invoice for the day's service (per-hour, per-event, or salary basis)
- Log expenses (groceries, travel, specialty items)
- Send invoice via email; accept payment via Venmo, Zelle, check, credit card, or wire
- Update client profile with new preferences, feedback, dietary changes
- Begin planning next client's service

### Breakpoints (Where Things Go Wrong)

| Breakpoint                             | What Happens                                                                                                                                                                        |
| -------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Client communication fragmentation** | Menu approvals, dietary changes, and preferences scattered across texts, emails, WhatsApp, and phone calls. Chefs scroll through dozens of messages to find what was agreed upon.   |
| **Last-minute dietary changes**        | Guest added day-of with severe allergy. Chef may not be able to safely accommodate. No system tracks this risk.                                                                     |
| **Scheduling conflicts**               | Two clients want the same day/time slot. No automated waitlist or conflict resolution. Lost revenue from turned-away clients.                                                       |
| **Shopping list errors**               | Fragmented recipe/client data leads to forgotten ingredients or wrong quantities. One mistake means a return trip to the store mid-cook.                                            |
| **Profitability blindness**            | No clear view of which clients/events are profitable after accounting for groceries, travel, time. Chefs underprice without knowing it.                                             |
| **Price tracking**                     | Ingredient prices change constantly. Spreadsheet-based costing goes stale immediately. No connection between what was actually paid at the store and what the recipe "should" cost. |
| **Recipe scaling**                     | Paper recipes aren't scaled or converted for different party sizes. Manual math under time pressure leads to errors.                                                                |
| **Proposal turnaround**                | Creating proposals is time-intensive. Administrative backlog delays client acquisition.                                                                                             |
| **Review/referral gap**                | Only ~25% of clients write reviews. No systematic way to capture social proof.                                                                                                      |

### Current Workarounds (What Tools/Hacks They Use)

| Task                | Common Workaround                                                                      |
| ------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Menu/recipe storage | Google Docs, Word documents, Manila folders with paper recipes, phone notes            |
| Client tracking     | Phone contacts with notes, spreadsheets, memory                                        |
| Shopping lists      | Handwritten, printed from recipe software, phone notes                                 |
| Invoicing           | QuickBooks, Venmo requests, Excel, generic invoice templates (ClickUp, PDF generators) |
| Proposals           | Word docs, PDF attachments, email drafts                                               |
| Payment collection  | Venmo, Zelle, personal check, cash, credit card, wire transfer                         |
| Communication       | Text, email, WhatsApp, phone calls (no unified thread per client)                      |
| Scheduling          | Google Calendar, paper planner, memory                                                 |
| Recipe costing      | Excel spreadsheets, manual calculation, "mental math"                                  |
| Marketing/CRM       | MailChimp, Infusionsoft, social media, word of mouth                                   |
| Accounting          | QuickBooks, bookkeeper (outsourced)                                                    |

### Missing Pieces (What No Tool Solves Well)

1. **Unified client thread**: No tool stitches together a client's texts, emails, dietary notes, past menus, payment history, and upcoming services into one view. Chefs cobble this from 5+ apps.

2. **Shopping list to actual cost tracking**: No tool connects "what I planned to buy" with "what I actually spent" at the register. Receipt scanning for ingredient-level price tracking barely exists for independent chefs.

3. **Real-time recipe costing with live prices**: Spreadsheets go stale. Software requires manual price updates. No tool automatically reflects what ingredients actually cost this week at the stores the chef uses.

4. **Proposal-to-invoice pipeline**: Creating a proposal, getting approval, converting it to a confirmed booking, tracking deposits, sending a final invoice, and recording payment is done across 3-5 separate tools. No single flow.

5. **Client dietary change propagation**: When a client updates their restrictions, that change should ripple through future menus, shopping lists, and recipes. Instead, it lives in a text message the chef might forget.

6. **Multi-client day optimization**: Chefs doing 2-3 clients per day have no tool that helps sequence shopping, cooking, and travel efficiently across clients.

7. **Revenue per client visibility**: After groceries, travel, and time, which clients are actually profitable? No tool makes this obvious without manual spreadsheet work.

## Persona 2: Executive Chef (Private Events / Catering Context)

### Real Daily/Weekly Workflow (Step by Step)

**1. Event Pipeline Management**

- Receive event inquiries (corporate dinners, weddings, private parties, hotel F&B events)
- Assess event scope: guest count, dietary needs, venue constraints, budget
- Create custom menu proposals with pricing (food cost + labor cost per dish)
- Negotiate with client; revise proposals through multiple rounds

**2. Menu Costing and Financial Planning**

- Cost every recipe: ingredient costs, sub-recipe costs, yield calculations, waste factors
- Calculate labor cost per dish (prep time, skill level required, plating complexity)
- Set menu prices targeting 25-35% food cost percentage
- Track actual vs. projected food costs per event
- Manage vendor relationships and negotiate bulk pricing

**3. Staff Scheduling and Management**

- Build kitchen brigade for each event (sous chefs, line cooks, prep cooks, servers)
- Create staff schedules accounting for event timing, prep days, and service
- Handle hiring, training, discipline, and performance reviews
- Manage rising labor costs and high turnover (younger, less stable labor pool post-pandemic)

**4. Prep List Creation and Delegation**

- Break down each event menu into detailed prep lists
- Assign tasks to team members based on skill level
- Sequence prep across multiple days leading up to the event
- Manage prep for multiple simultaneous events

**5. Inventory and Purchasing**

- Monitor stock levels across proteins, produce, dry goods, specialty items
- Place orders with multiple vendors (produce distributor, meat purveyor, dry goods supplier, specialty items)
- Handle price volatility: protein prices, seasonal produce, tariff-affected imports
- Track waste daily, categorize reasons (spoilage, overproduction, prep errors)

**6. Event Execution**

- Oversee all culinary production: timing, quality, presentation, temperature
- Coordinate between kitchen and front-of-house
- Handle last-minute changes with composure
- Ensure food safety and HACCP compliance

**7. Post-Event Analysis**

- Reconcile actual food costs vs. projected
- Assess waste and identify improvement areas
- Collect client feedback
- Update pricing if ingredient costs have shifted

### Breakpoints (Where Things Go Wrong)

| Breakpoint                          | What Happens                                                                                                                                                               |
| ----------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Multi-event prep collision**      | Prep lists for 3 events overlap. Staff confused about which prep is for which event. Items get cross-contaminated or misallocated.                                         |
| **Sub-recipe cost cascading**       | House marinara goes into 5 dishes. When tomato prices spike, all 5 dish costs change. In spreadsheets, this is "a rat's nest of cross-references."                         |
| **Staff overproduction**            | New or undertrained staff over-prep ingredients. Specialty items for slow-moving dishes spoil. Training gaps directly translate to food waste and cost overruns.           |
| **Price volatility**                | 91% of operators report food cost increases. Ingredient prices change faster than menus can be repriced. Tariffs and supply chain disruptions make forecasting unreliable. |
| **Last-minute order disruption**    | Late orders disrupt existing prep schedules. Kitchen capacity is already allocated. Accepting the order means quality risk on everything.                                  |
| **Profitability per event opacity** | Without integrated software, many caterers can't see true profitability per event. Revenue looks good but margins are invisible.                                           |
| **Communication breakdown**         | Kitchen staff not talking to servers. Front-of-house doesn't know about 86'd items or timing changes.                                                                      |
| **Capacity planning failure**       | Certain time windows consistently overwhelm the kitchen. No historical data used for demand forecasting.                                                                   |

### Current Workarounds

| Task               | Common Workaround                                                                                  |
| ------------------ | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Recipe costing     | Excel spreadsheets (FoodCostChef, custom templates), dedicated software (meez, MarketMan, ChefTec) |
| Prep lists         | Handwritten on whiteboards, printed templates, spreadsheets, Toast POS prep list templates         |
| Inventory          | Manual counting, spreadsheets, dedicated inventory software (Supy, MarketMan)                      |
| Staff scheduling   | Paper schedules, Excel, generic scheduling apps (7shifts, HotSchedules)                            |
| Event management   | Tripleseat, Caterease, Total Party Planner, CaterZen                                               |
| Menu proposals     | Word/PDF templates, Curate, custom proposal software                                               |
| Vendor ordering    | Phone calls, emails, vendor-specific portals                                                       |
| Financial tracking | QuickBooks, POS system reports, manual reconciliation                                              |

### Missing Pieces

1. **Unified event-to-plate pipeline**: No single tool connects the event inquiry through menu proposal, costing, prep list generation, inventory pull, staff assignment, execution, and post-event financial reconciliation. Operators use 3-7 separate systems.

2. **Real-time cost recalculation**: When a vendor invoice comes in with different prices than expected, the impact on every affected menu item should recalculate automatically. Most systems require manual updates.

3. **Waste-to-forecast loop**: Daily waste tracking data should feed back into future prep quantity decisions. Currently this is "gut feel" for most operations.

4. **Cross-event inventory optimization**: When two events on the same weekend both need cilantro, the system should consolidate ordering. Instead, each event is planned in isolation.

5. **Staff skill-to-task matching**: No tool helps assign prep tasks based on actual staff capability. The sous chef carries this knowledge in their head.

6. **Sustainability tracking**: Waste, packaging, and donation flows are invisible. Growing client demand for sustainability metrics can't be met.

## Persona 3: Sous Chef (Supporting Role)

### Real Daily/Weekly Workflow (Step by Step)

**1. Morning Setup (Before Service)**

- Arrive early; review the day's menu and any changes from the executive chef
- Check inventory: what's on hand, what's short, what needs to be ordered
- Create or review prep lists for the day
- Assign prep tasks to line cooks and prep cooks based on priority and skill level
- Set up stations with tools, preparation sheets, and materials

**2. Prep Oversight (Pre-Service)**

- Supervise all prep work: cutting, portioning, marinating, par-cooking
- Ensure prep meets quality and consistency standards
- Translate executive chef's high-level menu vision into actionable prep steps for the team
- Identify and resolve bottlenecks before they cascade
- Manage timing: longest-lead items started first, everything converging for service

**3. Service Execution**

- Run the line or specific stations during peak service
- Step in to cook when volume overwhelms the team
- Coordinate timing between stations (grill, saute, garde manger, pastry)
- Bridge communication between executive chef and line cooks
- Handle 86'd items, substitutions, and timing adjustments in real time

**4. Inventory and Ordering**

- Monitor stock levels throughout the day
- Place orders with suppliers for next-day delivery
- Manage par levels to minimize both waste and stockouts
- Receive deliveries, verify quality and quantities, check invoices against orders

**5. Staff Training and Development**

- Train new hires on recipes, techniques, and kitchen procedures
- Demonstrate plating standards and portion control
- Mentor line cooks on career development
- Handle minor disciplinary issues before they escalate

**6. End-of-Day Close**

- Oversee kitchen cleaning and sanitation
- Verify all food is properly stored (labeled, dated, FIFO rotation)
- Update inventory counts
- Communicate any issues or needs to the executive chef for the next day
- Review prep needs for tomorrow; start next day's prep list

### Breakpoints (Where Things Go Wrong)

| Breakpoint                    | What Happens                                                                                                                                                                                                              |
| ----------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Prep list accuracy**        | Executive chef changes the menu; sous chef's prep list is now wrong. Staff preps items no longer needed. Waste and time lost.                                                                                             |
| **Verbal-only communication** | High-level directives passed verbally get misinterpreted. "Make extra stock" becomes the wrong type or quantity. No written trail.                                                                                        |
| **Inventory count friction**  | Manual counting is tedious, error-prone, and time-consuming. Staff rush through it, leading to inaccurate par levels and surprise stockouts during service.                                                               |
| **Multi-tasking overload**    | Sous chef simultaneously supervises prep, trains a new hire, takes a delivery, and answers the exec chef's questions. Balls get dropped.                                                                                  |
| **Knowledge hoarding**        | The sous chef is the only person who knows which cook can handle which station, what the real yield on the beef tenderloin is, and where the backup vanilla extract is stored. When they're absent, the kitchen stumbles. |
| **Cross-shift handoff**       | Information about what was prepped, what's running low, and what issues arose doesn't transfer cleanly between shifts. New shift starts blind.                                                                            |
| **Recipe version confusion**  | Multiple versions of the same recipe exist (laminated card at the station, binder in the office, PDF on the chef's phone). Staff follows the wrong one.                                                                   |

### Current Workarounds

| Task                | Common Workaround                                                                      |
| ------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Prep lists          | Handwritten on legal pads or whiteboards, printed from templates, shared Google Sheets |
| Station assignments | Verbal communication, whiteboard in the kitchen, memory                                |
| Inventory counting  | Clipboard and pen, spreadsheets, dedicated apps (rare)                                 |
| Recipe reference    | Laminated cards at stations, recipe binders, phone photos of recipes                   |
| Shift communication | Verbal handoff, sticky notes, group text messages                                      |
| Training            | Shadow shifts, verbal instruction, printed SOPs (inconsistently maintained)            |
| Ordering            | Phone calls to vendors, vendor apps/portals, text messages                             |

### Missing Pieces

1. **Living prep list**: A prep list that updates in real time when the menu changes, when inventory is received, or when a cook marks a task complete. Current prep lists are static snapshots that go stale immediately.

2. **Institutional knowledge capture**: The sous chef's mental database of staff capabilities, ingredient yields, vendor quirks, and kitchen shortcuts has no home. When they leave, all of it goes with them.

3. **Cross-shift visibility**: No lightweight tool shows what the previous shift accomplished, what's left to do, and what problems they encountered. Group texts are unsearchable and get buried.

4. **Real-time inventory during service**: During a busy service, knowing exactly what's left of each protein or sauce without physically checking the walk-in would prevent over-selling and 86 surprises.

5. **Task-to-skill matching**: No system helps assign prep tasks based on who can actually do what. The sous chef carries this map in their head and rebuilds it every time staff turns over.

6. **Recipe version control**: There's no "single source of truth" for recipes that stays in sync across the station card, the office binder, and the costing spreadsheet. Changes in one place don't propagate.

## Cross-Persona Patterns

### Universal Pain Points (All Three Personas Share These)

1. **Fragmentation**: Every chef persona cobbles together 5-10 tools that don't talk to each other. Data lives in silos. Context is lost between tools.

2. **Recipe costing is broken everywhere**: Spreadsheets don't scale. Prices go stale. Sub-recipes create maintenance nightmares. No tool connects actual purchase prices to recipe costs automatically.

3. **Administrative burden displaces cooking**: The universal complaint across all personas is that business operations (proposals, invoicing, scheduling, costing, communication) consume more time than the actual craft of cooking.

4. **Verbal/mental knowledge has no system home**: Client preferences, staff capabilities, ingredient yields, vendor relationships, pricing history: all live in someone's head or scattered across messages. When that person is unavailable, the operation degrades.

5. **Financial visibility is always lagging**: Whether it's per-client profitability (private chef), per-event margins (executive chef), or food cost percentage (sous chef managing waste), the numbers are always retrospective and require manual calculation.

6. **Communication is unstructured**: Texts, emails, WhatsApp, verbal, sticky notes. No audit trail. No searchable history. Critical information gets buried.

### Where ChefFlow Has Natural Alignment

| ChefFlow Capability                                | Persona Fit                                                   |
| -------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Client profiles with dietary tracking              | Private Chef (primary), Executive Chef (events)               |
| Event lifecycle management (inquiry to completion) | All three                                                     |
| Quote/proposal generation and approval workflow    | Private Chef, Executive Chef                                  |
| Recipe management with costing                     | All three                                                     |
| Ingredient price tracking (OpenClaw pipeline)      | All three                                                     |
| Invoice generation and payment tracking            | Private Chef (primary), Executive Chef                        |
| Ledger-based financial visibility                  | Private Chef (per-client P&L), Executive Chef (per-event P&L) |
| Document generation (contracts, menus)             | Private Chef, Executive Chef                                  |
| AI concierge (Remy) for client communication       | Private Chef (primary)                                        |

### Gaps Where ChefFlow Could Differentiate

1. **Shopping list generation from confirmed menus**: Auto-generate consolidated shopping lists from approved menus, organized by store section. No major competitor does this well for independent operators.

2. **Actual cost capture**: Connect what was planned (recipe cost) with what was spent (grocery receipt / vendor invoice). Close the loop between projected and actual food cost per event.

3. **Client communication hub**: One thread per client that captures texts, emails, menu approvals, dietary changes, and payment status. Replace the "scroll through 50 texts to find what they said about allergies" problem.

4. **Prep list generation from event menus**: Given a confirmed menu and guest count, auto-generate scaled prep lists with task assignments. Currently manual everywhere.

5. **Multi-client day planning**: For private chefs serving 2-3 clients per day, optimize the shopping/cooking/travel sequence. Nobody solves this.

6. **Staff capability tracking**: Simple skill matrix that helps assign prep tasks. Replaces the sous chef's mental model.
