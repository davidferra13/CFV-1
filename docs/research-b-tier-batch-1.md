# B-Tier Vertical Research: Batch 1

Executive Chef | Sous Chef Stepping Out | Retreat/Wellness Chef | Yacht/Marine Chef
Research date: 2026-03-14

---

## 1. Executive Chef (Runs Kitchen, Doesn't Own the Business)

### 1.1 Who Are They?

Executive chefs manage the entire culinary operation of a restaurant, hotel, or food establishment without owning it. They are employees, not entrepreneurs.

- **Demographics:** 87% male, 56% are over 40, 56% white. 26% hold a bachelor's degree. There are approximately 137,000 executive chefs in the U.S. (OysterLink, 2025).
- **Revenue range:** $71,000 to $110,000 annually (national average), with a 75th percentile at $123,254. Top earners reach $155,025. Hotels and travel accommodations pay a median of $91,802; restaurants pay $85,304.
- **Team size:** Manage kitchen brigades of 5 to 50+ depending on establishment. Always have at least a sous chef and line cooks under them.
- **Geographic premium:** San Jose ($109,979), DC ($96,542), California ($96,176), Massachusetts ($94,894).

### 1.2 What's Their Workflow?

Executive chefs split time between administrative duties and kitchen operations.

- **Morning:** Arrive early (6-8am). Review inventory, check deliveries against orders, inspect ingredient quality. Plan daily specials based on what's available.
- **Mid-day:** Assign tasks to kitchen staff based on individual strengths. Oversee prep work across stations. Handle vendor calls, resolve supply issues.
- **Service:** Monitor every station during service. Inspect dishes before they leave the kitchen. Manage timing across the line. Handle kitchen-server coordination.
- **Post-service:** Review food cost reports, plan next day's orders, address staffing issues. Handle menu development and recipe testing during off-peak hours.
- **Administrative:** Budget management, hiring/training, menu engineering, food cost analysis, health code compliance, vendor negotiations. Some exec chefs spend more time on admin than cooking.

### 1.3 What Tools Do They Use Now?

- **Kitchen management:** JAMIX (2,500+ kitchens), xtraCHEF (real-time food cost), ChefTec/CorTec (inventory + costing), EZchef (Excel-based), meez (recipe management + scaling)
- **Ordering/inventory:** Sysco/US Foods ordering portals, CheddrSuite (links inventory to recipes), reciProfity
- **General:** POS systems (Toast, Square), spreadsheets (many still use Excel for food costing), paper checklists for prep lists and daily tasks, group texts and WhatsApp for staff communication
- **Pain point:** Only 13% of restaurant operators are fully satisfied with their current tech stack (RestroWorks, 2025). Critical information still "lives on scraps of paper, whiteboard notes, and text messages that disappear when needed most."

### 1.4 What Are They Complaining About?

- **Burnout is epidemic.** 58% of chefs report work-related mental health issues (anxiety, depression). 75% have contemplated leaving the industry entirely (Chef's Pencil survey). "Just under three-quarters" of one network's chef connections have left formal kitchen environments.
- **System integration failure.** "One system says you have 20 pounds of salmon and another shows 15, and you don't know which to trust for orders."
- **Kitchen-server conflicts.** "Tickets get lost, modified orders don't reach the line, and servers blame cooks (or vice versa) for mistakes."
- **Software learning curves.** Implementation takes 3-6 months. Dedicated staff time needed to maintain recipe and inventory data. Mobile experiences don't match desktop functionality.
- **Ownership captures value.** Exec chefs create the menus, build the teams, and drive the culture, but owners capture the financial upside. Bad management is cited as a top reason chefs leave.

### 1.5 What Competitor Tools Exist?

| Tool             | Focus                                                        | Price Range      |
| ---------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ | ---------------- |
| meez             | Recipe management, scaling, costing                          | Free tier + paid |
| xtraCHEF         | Automated food cost, invoice processing                      | $$$ (enterprise) |
| JAMIX            | Full kitchen intelligence (recipe + inventory + procurement) | $$$              |
| ChefTec          | Inventory, recipe costing, menu analysis                     | $$               |
| EZchef           | Excel-based food cost + menu engineering                     | $                |
| CheddrSuite      | Inventory linked to recipes                                  | $$               |
| Galley Solutions | Menu management, food costing for large operations           | $$$              |
| Toast/Square     | POS with basic kitchen display                               | $-$$$            |

Most of these tools are designed for restaurant owners or multi-unit operators, not for the exec chef personally. No tool helps an exec chef manage their own career, build a portfolio, or transition to independence.

### 1.6 What's Broken? (Top 5 Pain Points)

1. **No personal career tool.** Everything is tied to the restaurant. When a chef leaves, they lose access to all recipes, vendor contacts, food cost data, and operational knowledge they built.
2. **Recipe IP is unprotected.** Chefs develop menus and recipes but have no portable, personal recipe management system. The restaurant owns the POS, the ordering system, and often claims ownership of recipes.
3. **Financial invisibility.** Exec chefs manage food costs and labor but rarely see P&L in real time. They operate blind compared to owners.
4. **Fragmented communication.** Staff scheduling, prep lists, vendor orders, and service notes live in 4+ different systems (or on paper). Nothing talks to anything else.
5. **No path-to-independence toolkit.** Many exec chefs want to go freelance (consulting, private chef, pop-ups) but have no system to manage clients, invoices, or bookings outside the restaurant context.

### 1.7 What Would Make Them Switch?

- A personal "chef operating system" that travels with them between jobs, keeping recipes, vendor contacts, food cost benchmarks, and career history in one place
- Recipe management that's truly theirs (not the restaurant's), with scaling, costing, and portfolio features
- A bridge tool: helps them perform their current exec chef duties AND positions them for independence if/when they leave
- Mobile-first (they're on their feet all day, never at a desk)

### 1.8 How Do They Find Clients?

Executive chefs don't find clients in the traditional sense; they find employers. Channels include:

- **Recruiters:** Hospitality-specific agencies (Gecko Hospitality, Hospitalio, iHireChefs)
- **Networking:** Industry events, chef associations (ACF), word of mouth within the restaurant community
- **Job boards:** Indeed, Culinary Agents, Poached Jobs
- **Reputation:** Awards, media coverage, social media presence (Instagram is the chef's portfolio)
- **For side work:** Word of mouth for private dinners, pop-ups, catering gigs. Platforms like Upwork and Thumbtack for consulting.

### 1.9 How Do They Handle Money?

As employees, exec chefs receive W-2 salaries. For side work:

- **Invoicing:** Generic tools (QuickBooks, Wave, PDF templates). No chef-specific invoicing.
- **Side gig payments:** Venmo, Zelle, checks, cash. Often informal.
- **No deposit system:** Side gigs rarely use structured deposits or payment terms.
- **Tipping:** Not standard for exec chefs (unlike servers), though private dinner side gigs sometimes receive tips.

### 1.10 What's Their Relationship With Tech?

- **Phone-first, but not by choice.** They're on their feet 12+ hours. Can't sit at a desk. Phone is the only option.
- **Moderate adoption.** 76% of restaurant operators believe tech gives them a competitive advantage, but training and implementation are constant barriers.
- **Frustrated, not resistant.** They want tools that work; they just haven't found ones that solve their specific problems without creating new ones.
- **Instagram-savvy.** Many exec chefs maintain active social media for personal brand and career positioning.

## 2. Sous Chef Stepping Out (Transitioning From Employee to Independent)

### 2.1 Who Are They?

Sous chefs (or experienced line cooks) leaving restaurant employment to start their own personal/private chef business or freelance culinary career.

- **Demographics:** Typically 28-40 years old. 5-15 years of professional kitchen experience. Mostly trained on the job (some culinary school). Majority male, but the independent/personal chef space skews more female than restaurant kitchens.
- **Business size:** Solo operator (at least initially). No employees, no commercial kitchen, no restaurant lease.
- **Revenue range (Year 1):** $40,000-$62,000 realistic for a home-based start. "10 meals for four people per week brings in $62,000 in annual revenue with nearly $44,000 in profit, assuming a 70% margin" (Business Plan Suite). Scaled operations with a commercial kitchen can reach $250,000 annual revenue.
- **Startup costs:** As low as $500 for a minimal home-based model. Business license ($25-$500), liability insurance ($20-$50/month), basic equipment ($3,000-$7,000 if buying new). Marketing: $500-$1,500/month for digital presence.

### 2.2 What's Their Workflow?

The transition workflow is fundamentally different from restaurant work:

- **Client acquisition:** Constant hustle. Social media posting, word of mouth, networking at farmers markets, offering free tastings to friends of friends.
- **Consultation:** Meet potential clients, discuss dietary needs, preferences, allergies. Build menu proposals.
- **Shopping:** 2-4 hours per client booking. Farmers markets, specialty stores, wholesale suppliers. Travel time is significant.
- **Prep and cooking:** Cook in client's home (most common) or prep in a commercial kitchen and deliver. Adapt to unfamiliar kitchens every time. "Cooking in your home or at a restaurant is so different from cooking in other people's houses."
- **Cleanup and packaging:** Leave the client's kitchen cleaner than you found it. Package and label meals for the week.
- **Admin:** Invoicing, bookkeeping, scheduling, menu planning, grocery cost tracking. All done manually or with generic tools.
- **Weekly rhythm:** Typically serve 3-5 clients per week, each getting a full day of cooking (meal prep) or a single dinner event.

### 2.3 What Tools Do They Use Now?

- **Scheduling:** Google Calendar, phone calendar, or paper planners
- **Invoicing:** QuickBooks, Wave, generic invoice templates, or (commonly) nothing formal
- **Recipes:** Personal notebooks, Notes app, meez (if they know about it), Google Docs
- **Client communication:** Text messages, email, Instagram DMs
- **Grocery tracking:** Mental math, paper lists, or basic spreadsheet
- **Booking:** No dedicated system. "Can you do next Tuesday?" via text.
- **Contracts:** Most don't use them. Those who do use generic templates.

### 2.4 What Are They Complaining About?

- **Undercharging is the #1 mistake.** "One of the most common pitfalls for new freelancers is undercharging; don't just calculate time spent cooking, factor in planning, shopping, travel, and clean-up."
- **No business training.** Culinary school teaches cooking, not running a business. "Being a freelance chef is a business, and if you're not ready for the non-cooking parts, it can catch you off guard."
- **Client acquisition is exhausting.** "Until you have a regular clientele, it may be difficult earning an adequate amount of money."
- **Schedule conflicts.** "Both want you to cook dinner for them on Mondays." No system to manage availability or prevent double-booking.
- **Legal confusion.** Requirements differ by location. Food safety certifications, business licenses, insurance, cottage food laws, commercial kitchen requirements. "Educating yourself on the laws in your area is extremely important."
- **Isolation.** Going from a kitchen brigade to working alone. No sous chef, no line cooks, no team energy.

### 2.5 What Competitor Tools Exist?

| Tool              | Focus                                   | Fit for Stepping-Out Chef                      |
| ----------------- | --------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------- |
| meez              | Recipe management + scaling             | Good for recipes, doesn't cover business ops   |
| Kosmo             | Freelancer invoicing + expense tracking | Generic freelancer tool, not chef-specific     |
| Ontraport         | Marketing + CRM                         | Overkill for a solo chef starting out          |
| Chefpreneur       | Comprehensive chef business management  | Most relevant, but limited market presence     |
| Cast Iron         | Home chef e-commerce                    | For selling meals online, not service-based    |
| HoneyBook/Dubsado | Service-based business CRM              | Used by some personal chefs, not chef-specific |
| More Pleaze       | Chef marketplace (Hawaii)               | Regional, marketplace model                    |
| yhangry           | Private chef marketplace (UK)           | Marketplace, takes commission                  |

**Gap:** No single tool handles the full journey from "I just quit the restaurant" to "I have a thriving personal chef business." The existing tools either handle recipes OR business ops OR marketing, never all three.

### 2.6 What's Broken? (Top 5 Pain Points)

1. **No business-in-a-box for new chef entrepreneurs.** They need invoicing, scheduling, client management, recipe costing, and menu proposals in one place. Currently using 5+ separate tools (or nothing).
2. **Pricing is guesswork.** No tool helps them calculate true cost per meal including travel, shopping time, prep, cooking, cleanup, and overhead. They consistently undercharge.
3. **No client portal.** Clients text about allergies, email about dates, and call about menus. Nothing is centralized. Dietary restrictions get lost in text threads.
4. **No financial visibility.** They can't easily see "I made $4,200 this month but spent $1,800 on groceries and $400 on gas, so my actual take-home is $2,000."
5. **The confidence gap.** No tool helps them present professionally. They send texts instead of proposals, Venmo requests instead of invoices, and verbal agreements instead of contracts.

### 2.7 What Would Make Them Switch?

- "If I could send a client a professional menu proposal with pricing and have them book and pay a deposit, all from my phone, I'd pay for that."
- A tool that makes them look established even when they're just starting out
- Automated grocery cost tracking that connects to their recipes
- Client intake forms that capture allergies, preferences, and kitchen details before the first visit
- Simple financial dashboard: revenue, expenses, profit, broken down by client

### 2.8 How Do They Find Clients?

- **Word of mouth (dominant).** Friends, family, former restaurant regulars, neighbors. "Cook for one family, they tell three more."
- **Social media:** Instagram (food photos), TikTok (cooking videos), Facebook community groups
- **Marketplaces:** Take a Chef, yhangry (UK), More Pleaze (Hawaii), Thumbtack, Bark
- **Networking:** Farmers markets, food events, local business groups, real estate agents (for luxury homeowners)
- **Referral partnerships:** Wedding planners, event coordinators, real estate agents, wellness practitioners

### 2.9 How Do They Handle Money?

- **Invoicing:** PDF templates, QuickBooks (if sophisticated), Venmo/Zelle requests (common), or verbal "just pay me after"
- **Deposits:** Rare among new chefs. Those who do charge 50% upfront learned it the hard way (no-shows, cancellations).
- **Payment terms:** Usually day-of or within a few days. Grocery costs often billed separately.
- **Line items:** Groceries, meal prep labor, service hours, mileage. Most don't itemize clearly.
- **Tipping:** Common for event work (10-20%), less common for weekly meal prep clients.
- **Tax tracking:** Minimal. Many don't track expenses properly and get surprised at tax time.

### 2.10 What's Their Relationship With Tech?

- **Phone-first by nature.** They run their entire business from their phone already (texts, Instagram, Venmo).
- **Not tech-resistant, but tech-overwhelmed.** They don't know which tools to pick, can't afford enterprise software, and don't have time to learn complex systems.
- **Instagram is their storefront.** If they're not on Instagram, they barely exist to potential clients.
- **Will pay for simplicity.** They'll pay $20-50/month for something that saves them 5+ hours of admin per week. They won't pay for something that takes 3 months to set up.

## 3. Retreat/Wellness Chef (Yoga Retreats, Wellness Centers, Spas)

### 3.1 Who Are They?

Chefs who specialize in cooking for wellness retreats, yoga retreats, meditation centers, and spa programs. They operate at the intersection of culinary arts and wellness culture.

- **Demographics:** Skews female compared to restaurant kitchens. Typically 30-50 years old. Often have personal wellness practices (yoga, meditation, plant-based eating). Many are self-taught or have non-traditional culinary backgrounds (nutrition certifications, holistic health training, Ayurvedic cooking).
- **Business size:** Almost always solo. May hire 1-2 assistants for larger retreats (20+ guests). Seasonal and project-based.
- **Revenue range:** Highly variable. A single 4-day retreat for 13 people can gross $3,000-$8,000 (ChefTalk forum discussion). Day rates of $1,500+ for groups of 6+. Top retreat chefs charge $120+/person for dinner, $75+/person for lunch. Experienced retreat chefs doing 10-15 retreats per year can gross $75,000-$150,000, but income is lumpy and seasonal.
- **Unique characteristic:** Many retreat chefs also offer other services (nutrition consulting, cooking classes, wellness coaching, recipe development) to fill gaps between retreat bookings.

### 3.2 What's Their Workflow?

Retreat chef work is project-based, not daily:

- **Pre-retreat (2-8 weeks before):**
  - Consult with retreat organizer on theme, dietary philosophy, group size, budget
  - Collect dietary restrictions via registration forms (vegan, gluten-free, Ayurvedic, raw, keto, allergies)
  - Design multi-day menu that aligns with retreat theme and accommodates all restrictions
  - Source ingredients (local, organic, seasonal preferred). Coordinate delivery to retreat location.
  - Create prep timeline and shopping lists

- **During retreat (3-7 days typical):**
  - Cook 3 meals per day plus snacks/juices/teas for 8-30 guests
  - Manage multiple simultaneous dietary restrictions at every meal
  - Label all meals clearly with allergen info
  - Use dedicated prep zones to prevent cross-contamination
  - Adapt menus based on guest feedback and ingredient availability
  - Work alone or with 1 assistant in an unfamiliar kitchen

- **Post-retreat:**
  - Invoice the retreat organizer
  - Document recipes and menus for portfolio
  - Solicit testimonials
  - Market for next booking

### 3.3 What Tools Do They Use Now?

- **Menu planning:** Google Docs, printed spreadsheets, personal recipe binders
- **Dietary tracking:** Spreadsheets or paper lists. "Information would just be handed out as a printout to everybody, with nobody being relayed information as it changed."
- **Booking:** Email, phone calls, direct relationships with retreat organizers. No booking platform.
- **Invoicing:** QuickBooks, Wave, or manual invoices via email
- **Communication:** Email threads with retreat organizers. "Scattered communication leads to missed messages and frustration."
- **Retreat management platforms (organizer side):** WeTravel, Retreat Guru, BookingLayer, SquadTrip. These are for the retreat organizer, not the chef.
- **Social media:** Instagram, personal website showcasing retreat menus and food photography

### 3.4 What Are They Complaining About?

- **Dietary restriction overload.** Every retreat has 5-10 different dietary needs (vegan, GF, nut-free, nightshade-free, Ayurvedic, raw, low-FODMAP). Managing them all simultaneously without a system is stressful and error-prone.
- **Unfamiliar kitchens.** Every retreat center has a different kitchen setup. Equipment varies wildly. No standardization.
- **Income instability.** Retreats are seasonal (peak: spring and fall). Summer and winter can be dead months. No recurring revenue.
- **Communication chaos.** Multiple email threads with organizers, attendees, and staff. Dietary changes come in last-minute. "A clunky registration process can frustrate potential attendees before they even step foot into your retreat."
- **Isolation and physical demands.** Cooking solo for 20+ people, 3 meals a day, for multiple days straight. No kitchen brigade support.
- **Undervaluation.** Retreat organizers often have tight budgets and expect the chef to absorb grocery cost overruns.

### 3.5 What Competitor Tools Exist?

| Tool         | Focus                                   | Fit for Retreat Chef                                       |
| ------------ | --------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------- |
| BookingLayer | Retreat booking + registration          | For organizers, not chefs                                  |
| Retreat Guru | Retreat directory + management software | For organizers, not chefs                                  |
| WeTravel     | Group travel payments + planning        | For organizers, not chefs                                  |
| SquadTrip    | Retreat planning app                    | For organizers, not chefs                                  |
| meez         | Recipe management                       | Good for recipes, doesn't handle retreat-specific workflow |
| Regpack      | Registration + payments                 | Generic, not food-focused                                  |

**Critical gap:** Every retreat management tool is built for the retreat organizer (the yoga teacher, the wellness coach). None are built for the chef. The chef is always an afterthought in the retreat tech stack.

### 3.6 What's Broken? (Top 5 Pain Points)

1. **No dietary restriction management system.** Tracking 15 different dietary needs across 3 meals per day for 5 days is currently done on paper or spreadsheets. One mistake (missed allergy) could be life-threatening.
2. **No retreat-specific menu planning tool.** Regular recipe software doesn't handle "make this vegan AND gluten-free AND nut-free while scaling for 22 people and substituting local ingredients."
3. **No chef-side booking system.** Retreat chefs manage availability, quotes, and contracts via email. Double-bookings happen. There's no way for organizers to see availability or request a quote without a phone call.
4. **No financial tracking for project-based work.** Each retreat is a separate project with separate grocery costs, travel expenses, and labor. No tool aggregates this into "how much did I actually make on that retreat?"
5. **No portfolio/marketplace.** Retreat organizers find chefs through word of mouth or Google. There's no dedicated platform connecting retreat chefs with organizers.

### 3.7 What Would Make Them Switch?

- A dietary restriction manager that takes intake form data and auto-generates compatible menus
- Menu planning that handles multi-day, multi-restriction meal planning with scaling and substitutions
- A booking calendar where retreat organizers can see availability and request quotes
- Per-project P&L tracking (revenue minus groceries minus travel minus assistant pay = actual profit)
- A portfolio page that showcases past retreats, menus, and testimonials

### 3.8 How Do They Find Clients?

- **Direct relationships (dominant).** Most retreat chefs work with the same 3-10 retreat organizers repeatedly. One chef reported catering 75 retreats over 10 years, largely through repeat relationships.
- **Personal websites/portfolios.** Essential for credibility. Showcase past retreat menus, food photography, dietary specialties.
- **Instagram.** Visual platform perfect for showcasing beautiful, wellness-aligned food.
- **RetreatHub job board.** Connects chefs with retreat centers hiring seasonal or contract cooks.
- **Word of mouth in wellness community.** Yoga teacher networks, wellness practitioner circles, retreat center staff recommendations.
- **Specialization marketing.** Chefs who specialize (Ayurvedic, raw food, plant-based, Hawaiian medicinal food) attract niche retreat organizers.

### 3.9 How Do They Handle Money?

- **Pricing models:** Per-person-per-day (most common), day rate + groceries (second most common), or all-inclusive per-person-per-meal.
- **Typical rates:** $75-$120+/person for dinner, $1,500/day for groups of 6+, or $5,000+ for a 2-day retreat.
- **Deposits:** Usually 50% upfront, remainder on first day of retreat. More formalized than personal chef work because retreat organizers expect contracts.
- **Grocery billing:** Either included in the per-person rate or billed separately with receipts. Professional provisioners charge ~20% markup.
- **Payment methods:** Bank transfer, check, or PayPal. Venmo for smaller operations.
- **Contracts:** More common than in personal chef work because retreat organizers are often businesses themselves.

### 3.10 What's Their Relationship With Tech?

- **Wellness-oriented, not tech-oriented.** Many retreat chefs chose this path specifically to escape corporate/restaurant culture. They value simplicity and mindfulness over optimization.
- **Instagram-comfortable.** They use social media well for marketing and portfolio display.
- **Phone-first.** Working in remote retreat locations (mountains, beaches, rural areas) means laptop access is limited. Everything needs to work on mobile.
- **Will adopt tools that reduce stress.** They're not anti-tech; they're anti-complexity. A simple, beautiful tool that handles dietary restrictions and menu planning would get immediate adoption.
- **Low tolerance for enterprise software.** They're solo operators. If setup takes more than 30 minutes, they'll quit.

## 4. Yacht/Marine Chef (Private Boats, Charter Vessels)

### 4.1 Who Are They?

Professional chefs who work aboard private yachts and charter vessels, cooking for owners, guests, and crew in extremely constrained galley environments.

- **Demographics:** Primarily 25-45 years old. International workforce (Australian, South African, British, American, Filipino chefs are common). Most come from fine dining or hotel backgrounds. Gender split is more balanced than restaurant kitchens.
- **Business size:** Always solo (sole chef on smaller yachts) or head chef with 1-2 sous chefs on larger vessels (60m+).
- **Revenue range:**
  - Below 40m yacht: EUR 4,000-7,000/month (~$52,000-$91,000/year)
  - 40-60m yacht: EUR 6,500-9,500+/month (~$85,000-$124,000/year)
  - Above 60m yacht (head chef): EUR 7,500-14,000+/month (~$98,000-$182,000/year)
  - U.S. average: $90,386/year (ZipRecruiter). Florida: $82,150. California: $100,324.
  - Charter tips add 5-15% of charter fee, split among crew. On a $100,000/week charter, the chef's tip share can be $2,000-$5,000.
- **Key distinction:** Private yacht chefs are salaried employees of the yacht owner. Charter yacht chefs work vessel-to-vessel, more like contractors.

### 4.2 What's Their Workflow?

Yacht chef work is uniquely intense and constrained:

- **6:00 AM:** Start in galley. Bake fresh croissants, muffins, scones, breads. Prepare crew breakfast spread. Set up guest breakfast (often served 8-10 AM).
- **Morning:** Prepare lunch for guests (3-12+ people) and crew (5-20 people). Two separate menus at different quality levels.
- **Afternoon:** Prep for dinner. Plan next day's menus based on remaining provisions. Manage afternoon snacks, canapes for sundowner cocktails.
- **Evening:** Execute fine-dining-quality dinner service. Often 5-7 courses for guests. Separate crew dinner.
- **Night:** Clean galley thoroughly, prep pastries for next morning, run dishwasher, organize storage. Bed by 10:30 PM.
- **Hours:** 18-20 hour days during charter are common. "More grueling than restaurant work." Some sole chefs report consistent 20-hour days.
- **Provisioning:** Major task between charters. Calculate provisions for 12+ guests and crew for 10-14 days. Source ingredients in foreign ports, often in languages you don't speak, in markets without card machines or receipts.

### 4.3 What Tools Do They Use Now?

- **Yacht management platforms:** IN-YACHT (provisioning module with cost comparison), DeepBlue (APA management, provisioning, expense tracking), Sealogical (740+ vessels, compliance-focused), YMS360
- **Recipe/menu:** Paprika Recipe Manager, personal recipe binders, WhatsApp groups for recipe sharing with other yacht chefs
- **Provisioning:** Spreadsheets (Excel/Google Sheets), paper lists, WhatsApp messages to provisioning agents. Professional provisioners charge ~20% markup.
- **APA tracking:** Spreadsheets or yacht management software. Must account for every dollar of the Advance Provisioning Allowance and return detailed reports to charter guests.
- **Communication:** WhatsApp (industry standard), yacht crew Facebook groups, Dockwalk forums
- **Job hunting:** Crew agencies (YPI Crew, Bluewater, CrewSeekers), Yotspot, Dockwalk, dockwalking (literally walking the docks with a resume)

### 4.4 What Are They Complaining About?

- **Provisioning in remote locations is brutal.** "In places like the Maldives where good-quality local produce is impossible to find and everything fresh has to be flown in, this adds stress, costs, and lots of planning." Chefs must "calculate provisions for 12 guests and crew for 10 or more days, often lugging everything around the market by themselves."
- **APA accounting is tedious.** Every purchase must be tracked, receipted, and reported. "Markets may not have card machines or receipts, requiring manual tracking of money."
- **Extreme hours with no relief.** "Eighteen to twenty-hour days, every day during charters." No days off during charter. Sole chefs have zero backup.
- **Tiny galley spaces.** "Compared to professional restaurant kitchens, superyacht galleys are strikingly tight." Equipment is world-class, but counter space and storage are minimal.
- **Guest dietary demands with no advance notice.** Charter guests may announce allergies, preferences, or dietary changes day-of. Provisioning was done a week ago based on different information.
- **Isolation.** Living and working in a confined space with the same small crew for months. No social life outside the boat.

### 4.5 What Competitor Tools Exist?

| Tool                | Focus                                      | Fit for Yacht Chef                                      |
| ------------------- | ------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------- |
| IN-YACHT            | Vessel management with provisioning module | Good provisioning features, but vessel-management-first |
| DeepBlue            | All-in-one yacht management, APA tracking  | Strong on APA, but designed for captains/managers       |
| Sealogical          | ISM compliance, crew scheduling            | Compliance-focused, not chef-focused                    |
| YMS360              | Yacht management by industry veterans      | General yacht ops, not galley-specific                  |
| Seahub              | Yacht maintenance software                 | Engineering-focused, no galley features                 |
| Paprika             | Recipe management (consumer)               | Good for personal recipe storage, no provisioning       |
| The Superyacht Chef | Provisioning guides, community, resources  | Content/community, not software                         |

**Critical gap:** Yacht management software handles provisioning as one small module within vessel operations. No tool is built from the chef's perspective. APA tracking, menu planning, dietary management, and provisioning are all separate workflows with no integration.

### 4.6 What's Broken? (Top 5 Pain Points)

1. **No integrated provisioning + menu + dietary system.** Chef plans menus, then creates shopping lists manually, then tracks costs manually, then reports APA manually. These are all the same workflow but live in different tools.
2. **APA accounting is still mostly spreadsheets.** Tracking every receipt, converting currencies, categorizing expenses, and producing guest-ready reports is hours of unpaid admin work.
3. **No dietary preference management for rotating guests.** Charter guests change every week. Each group has different allergies, preferences, and dietary philosophies. No system carries this information forward efficiently.
4. **Provisioning for remote locations has zero tech support.** When you're in the middle of the Mediterranean, you need to know what's available locally, what needs to be shipped, and what substitutions work. Currently all tribal knowledge.
5. **Career management is word-of-mouth only.** No portfolio system, no verified reviews, no way to build a professional reputation outside of agency databases and WhatsApp groups.

### 4.7 What Would Make Them Switch?

- A galley-first tool: menu planning + provisioning lists + APA tracking + dietary management, all connected
- Currency conversion and receipt scanning for APA reporting
- Offline functionality (essential; internet at sea is unreliable and expensive)
- A professional profile/portfolio that agencies and captains can review
- Provisioning guides by region: what's available where, local supplier contacts, seasonal availability

### 4.8 How Do They Find Clients?

- **Crew agencies (dominant).** YPI Crew, Bluewater, CrewSeekers, Cotton Crews, Palma Yacht Crew. Register with multiple agencies, maintain updated profiles with food photos, menu plans, certifications, and references.
- **Dockwalking.** Physically walking marina docks in yachting hubs (Antibes, Fort Lauderdale, Palma de Mallorca) with a printed CV and food portfolio. "A grueling task with lots of rejection."
- **Networking.** Yacht crew WhatsApp groups, Facebook groups, crew bars in yachting hubs. "Yacht chef Facebook groups are beneficial, allowing you to post for insight and advice."
- **Day work.** Short-term gigs found through websites, social media, WhatsApp groups, or dockwalking connections. Day work leads to permanent positions.
- **Yotspot/Dockwalk job boards.** Online platforms where captains and management companies post chef positions.
- **Repeat clients.** Once established, a good yacht chef gets called back by the same captain or owner for years.

### 4.9 How Do They Handle Money?

- **Salary:** Monthly, paid by the yacht owner or management company. Tax-free in many jurisdictions (Cayman Islands, Marshall Islands flagged vessels).
- **APA (Advance Provisioning Allowance):** 25-40% of charter fee, pre-funded by charter guests. Chef manages the food portion. Standard: sailing yachts 20-30%, motor yachts 30-40%. Must produce detailed expense reports. Unspent APA returned to guest or given as crew gratuity.
- **Tips:** 10-15% of base charter fee, split among all crew. On a $150,000/week charter, total tip pool might be $15,000-$22,500. Chef's share depends on crew size and hierarchy.
- **Invoicing:** Not applicable for salaried private yacht chefs. Freelance/rotational chefs invoice through their agency or directly.
- **No personal billing system needed** for most yacht chefs, since they're employees. The exception is rotational/freelance chefs who need contracts and invoicing.

### 4.10 What's Their Relationship With Tech?

- **Phone-dependent.** WhatsApp is the communication backbone of the entire yachting industry. Everything from job offers to provisioning orders to recipe sharing happens on WhatsApp.
- **Offline reality.** Internet at sea is satellite-based, expensive, and slow. Any tool must work offline or with intermittent connectivity.
- **Spreadsheet-competent.** Most yacht chefs can handle Excel/Sheets for provisioning and APA tracking. They're comfortable with structured data.
- **App-willing but not app-saturated.** They use a few key apps (WhatsApp, recipe apps, maybe yacht management software) but aren't overwhelmed by tech. Willing to adopt a good tool if it saves time on provisioning and APA.
- **Privacy-conscious.** Working for UHNW (ultra-high-net-worth) individuals means discretion is paramount. They're wary of tools that might expose client information.

### Universal Pain Points Across All Four Verticals

1. **Recipe ownership and portability.** Every vertical wants recipes that belong to THEM, not their employer or platform.
2. **Financial visibility.** Nobody can easily answer "how much did I actually make?" after accounting for all costs.
3. **Phone-first operation.** All four verticals work on their feet. Desktop-first software is a non-starter.
4. **Dietary restriction management.** From restaurant guests to retreat attendees to yacht charter guests, managing allergies and dietary needs is universal and universally manual.
5. **Professional presentation.** The ability to send polished proposals, menus, and invoices (instead of texts and Venmo requests) is valued across all verticals.
