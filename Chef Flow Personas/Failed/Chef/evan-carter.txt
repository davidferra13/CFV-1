Chef Profile: "Evan Carter" - Commis Chef in Michelin-Starred Kitchen (Converter)

I was trained in the U.S., and everything I know lives in cups, ounces, and Fahrenheit. Now I’m in a Michelin-starred kitchen in France where everything is grams, liters, and Celsius - and speed matters. I understand metric, but I don’t think in it yet, and that gap shows up every day.

Business Reality
Right now:

5–6 services per week (lunch + dinner in a Michelin-starred kitchen)

60–90 covers per service depending on season

Brigade of 12–18 chefs on the line

Responsible for 2–3 stations rotating weekly

100% of recipes, prep lists, and ordering in metric units

Personal notes and mental math still in imperial

10–20 on-the-fly conversions per shift under time pressure

Primary Failure: Real-Time Conversion Lag
Service doesn’t wait for me to convert. When I see 225g, my brain still wants ounces. When I hear 180°C, I translate to Fahrenheit before I act. That extra step costs seconds - and seconds matter here. I’ve over- or under-shot quantities because I rushed a conversion. It’s not a knowledge problem; it’s a reflex problem. My workaround is memorizing common conversions and writing cheat notes, but anything outside those patterns slows me down.

Structural Issue: Recipe Internalization Gap
Recipes are written in metric, but my intuition is imperial. I can follow instructions, but I don’t feel the quantities. In the U.S., I could eyeball and adjust. Here, I hesitate unless I measure precisely. That affects consistency and confidence. My workaround is over-measuring and double-checking, which adds friction during prep.

Structural Issue: Temperature & Timing Mismatch
Heat is where I feel it most. I think in Fahrenheit for searing, roasting, and baking cues. In Celsius, I need to translate or rely on learned equivalents. That slows decision-making when I need to adjust on the fly. My workaround is memorizing anchor points (e.g., 180°C ≈ 350°F), but anything in between requires thought.

Structural Issue: Procurement & Ordering Precision
Ordering and prep sheets are strictly metric. Small errors compound - ordering 2 kg vs 2.5 kg affects yield and cost. I’ve had near-misses because I converted incorrectly under pressure. My workaround is re-checking everything twice, which costs time and still isn’t foolproof.

Psychological Model
I optimize for: speed, precision, and not breaking flow during service
I refuse to compromise on: accuracy of quantities and consistency of output
I avoid: mental math during critical moments
I evaluate tools by: whether they eliminate conversion thinking entirely

Pass / Fail Conditions
For this system to work for me, it must:

Display quantities in metric with optional inline imperial equivalents so I can act without converting.

Provide real-time, context-aware conversions (weights, volumes, temperatures) without leaving the workflow.

Let me input or think in imperial while automatically storing and executing in metric.

Offer station-specific quick references for common quantities and temperatures used during service.

Normalize recipes so I can internalize proportions (ratios, percentages) independent of unit system.

Integrate with prep and ordering sheets to prevent quantity errors at the source.

Reduce conversion steps to zero during active service so I never have to pause to translate units.
