**Guest Profile: "Emma Chamberlain" - First-Time Private Dining Guest (Effortlessly Chic)**

I’m mostly in the creative space, doing a mix of brand partnerships and occasional appearances. My lifestyle is pretty low-key, but when I do commit to an event, it has to feel effortless and really good. I don't want to spend the evening worrying about logistics, the menu, or whether I'm in the right place. I just want to show up, have a drink, and talk to people.

### Business Reality

Right now:
* I attend **1 to 3** private dining, farm dinner, or ticketed events per **quarter**.
* My typical spend per event is around **$300 - $500** per person, including any paid tickets or menu pre-orders. I rarely spend more than **$600** in one night.
* I am very particular about dietary needs; I am **vegan** and usually have a sensitivity to **nuts** and **high-acid ingredients**.
* The last event I attended was at The Swift Bar in Los Angeles, on a Friday night in early November, for a group of **8** people.
* The communication setup is a chaotic mix: the initial invite was a beautiful, but vague, email link; I received a separate group text from the host with the actual address; and then I had to manually confirm my dietary restrictions via a separate Google Form link that expired after 72 hours.

---

### Primary Failure: The Pre-Event Information Overload

The single biggest pain point is the sheer fragmentation of the required information. Because the confirmation details are spread across three different channels—an initial email, a text message, and a separate form—I spend valuable time cross-referencing them. What this means is that if I need to change my RSVP status, or if the menu changes, I don't know *which* channel is the authoritative source. This creates unnecessary anxiety and forces me to dedicate mental energy to logistics instead of enjoying the experience. If the system doesn't provide one single, immediate source of truth for all event details, I risk showing up late, or worse, with the wrong expectations about the menu or seating.

---

### Structural Issue: RSVP Confirmation Drift

When I RSVP, I expect immediate, comprehensive confirmation, not a series of "pending" status updates. Currently, if I confirm via the main platform, but then the host has to manually adjust my ticket status (e.g., moving me from 'Confirmed' to 'Attending with special request'), I have to wait for a separate email or text to tell me the change. I often end up treating the initial platform confirmation as final, only to be surprised by conflicting information later. This forces me to rely on the host's personal calendar reminders or chasing down a coordinator, which is inefficient and embarrassing.

---

### Structural Issue: Dietary and Special Request Siloing

My dietary requirements are always documented in a separate field, usually a small text box on the RSVP form. This information never seems to flow seamlessly to the kitchen staff or the floor manager. I have often had to verbally remind the host of my restrictions *again* upon arrival, even though I put them down initially. This means that the operational system is failing to create a single, actionable record that moves from the guest's input to the kitchen's prep list. The current workaround is usually for the host to take down my allergy on a sticky note and physically hand it to the kitchen manager, which is fragile and prone to being lost.

---

### Structural Issue: The Post-Event Feedback Vacuum

The process for giving feedback is never integrated into the core event experience. After a fantastic night, I might think, "I loved the cocktail pairing, but the bread basket was too salty." I have to leave the venue, open a different browser, and search for the feedback form, which is often already closed or generic. There is no natural, low-friction way to provide immediate, actionable feedback about the service, the food, or the logistics right after the experience. This means the venue loses valuable real-time data, and I lose the convenience of completing the loop.

---

### Structural Issue: Ticket and Seating Ambiguity

If the event has paid tickets, the system needs to clearly differentiate between the *ticket purchased* and the *allocated seat*. Sometimes, the booking platform shows my name, but the actual physical seating chart provided by the venue is different, and I am told to meet someone in a different room entirely. The current workaround is often a physical, printed seating chart handed out at the door, which is easily lost or discarded. The system needs to manage the physical, real-world mapping of the reservation.

---

### Psychological Model

I optimize for: **Seamlessness and Authenticity**. The experience must feel effortless, beautiful, and utterly natural.
I refuse to compromise on: **Respect for my time and my dietary needs**. Nothing can feel like administrative homework.
I avoid: **Complex forms, multi-step processes, or needing to switch between 3 or more different digital interfaces** just to confirm one piece of information.
I evaluate tools by: **How little mental energy they require.** If I have to think about the process, it's too much effort.

I make decisions based on the overall aesthetic and the perceived ease of the process. If the booking system feels clunky, confusing, or requires me to jump through too many hoops, I assume the experience itself will be equally clunky, and I'll be annoyed.

---

### Pass / Fail Conditions

For this system to work for me, it must:

1. The system must **provide a single, persistent, and easily accessible source of truth** for all event details (date, time, location, dress code, menu highlights) that remains consistent regardless of the communication channel.
2. The system must **automatically push all critical updates** (e.g., menu changes, seating adjustments, cancellation notices) to my preferred contact method (SMS/Email) and highlight the change clearly against the existing booking record.
3. The system must **manage the full lifecycle of special requests**, ensuring that my specific dietary restrictions are visible and confirmed to the kitchen staff and the service team *at least* 48 hours before the event, and that this confirmation is visible to me.
4. The system must **integrate the payment and ticket status** with the final allocated seating chart, and be able to display a single, definitive confirmation that includes both my seat number and the final menu package I purchased.
5. The system must **allow for immediate, integrated feedback submission** directly through the confirmation link or a follow-up email, allowing me to rate specific elements like the cocktail pairing or the quality of the bread, while simultaneously flagging the venue manager.
6. The system must **allow the host to view my entire guest profile** (RSVP history, dietary notes, previous feedback) in one dashboard view, so they don't have to cross-reference three different spreadsheets or notes.
7. The system must **handle the cancellation or modification of my reservation** with clear, automated communication of any financial implications (deposits, non-refundable fees) immediately upon the host's action.
