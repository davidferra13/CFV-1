**Chef Profile: "Evan Carter" - Commis Chef in Michelin-Starred Kitchen (Converter)**

I was trained in the U.S., and everything I know lives in cups, ounces, and Fahrenheit. Now I’m in a Michelin-starred kitchen in France where everything is grams, liters, and Celsius—and precision is non-negotiable. I understand metric, but I don’t *think* in it yet, and that gap shows up in the smallest, most exacting ways.

### Business Reality

Right now:

* 5–6 services per week (lunch + dinner in a Michelin-starred kitchen)
* 60–90 covers per service depending on season
* Brigade of 12–18 chefs on the line
* Responsible for 2–3 stations rotating weekly
* 100% of recipes, prep lists, and ordering in metric units
* Personal notes and intuition still rooted in imperial
* 10–20 on-the-fly conversions per shift under time pressure

### Primary Failure: Precision Conflict Between Systems

The issue isn’t just converting—it’s that the numbers don’t *translate cleanly*. In my head, roasting chicken at 350°F is normal. Here, it’s 180°C. Fine. But when I convert that back, it’s 356°F—not a number I would ever naturally use. So now I’m stuck between two systems where neither one aligns perfectly with the other. If I round to 350°F, it’s technically wrong. If I say 356°F, it feels unnatural. And in this kitchen, “close enough” is not a philosophy—it’s a mistake. My workaround is memorizing anchor points, but the mismatch never fully resolves, and it creates constant low-level friction.

### Structural Issue: Non-Intuitive Temperature Mapping

Temperature is where the disconnect becomes obvious. I don’t just execute temperatures—I *feel* them. Searing, roasting, baking—I know how heat behaves in Fahrenheit. In Celsius, I’m translating behavior, not just numbers. And the conversions don’t land on clean, intuitive values. 160°C, 170°C, 185°C—they all map to strange Fahrenheit numbers that I would never write down. My workaround is approximating internally, but approximation conflicts with the level of precision expected here.

### Structural Issue: Mental Model Misalignment

Metric is built on clean scaling—grams, liters, percentages. Imperial is not. My instincts are shaped by volume (cups, tablespoons) and rough visual estimation. Here, everything is weight-based and exact. Even when I follow recipes perfectly, I don’t *internalize* them the same way. I’m executing instructions instead of understanding them at a reflex level. My workaround is taking detailed notes and repeating tasks, but it’s a slower path to fluency.

### Structural Issue: Conversion Overload in Motion

During prep and service, I’m constantly bridging two systems in my head. A chef calls out 225g—I translate to ounces. I check a temperature—I convert to Fahrenheit. That extra step compounds throughout the day. It’s not catastrophic, but it adds hesitation, and hesitation is visible in a kitchen like this. My workaround is building mental shortcuts, but anything outside those patterns slows me down.

### Psychological Model

I optimize for: exactness, consistency, and internal clarity
I refuse to compromise on: precision, even at the level of a few degrees or grams
I avoid: rounding, guessing, or “close enough” conversions
I evaluate tools by: whether they eliminate translation and let me operate in one coherent system

### Pass / Fail Conditions

For this system to work for me, it must:

1. Present temperatures and quantities in metric while optionally showing exact imperial equivalents (not rounded estimates).
2. Eliminate the need for mental conversion by allowing me to think in imperial while executing in metric.
3. Provide precise, non-rounded conversions (e.g., 180°C = 356°F) when needed, without forcing approximation.
4. Help me internalize metric relationships (ratios, scaling, heat behavior) so I stop relying on translation.
5. Integrate seamlessly into recipes, prep lists, and station workflows without adding extra steps.
6. Allow me to annotate and build my own reference system to bridge intuition over time.
7. Remove hesitation during service by making all unit interactions immediate and unambiguous.
