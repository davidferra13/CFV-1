**Chef Profile: "Julian" Thorne - Head Chef (Purist Operator)**

I run a restaurant that is less a business and more a conversation with the land. We don't just serve food; we tell the story of the place we are in, right now. My entire operation hinges on the quality and variability of ingredients sourced within a 10-mile radius. Everything—from the salinity of the salt to the depth of the root vegetables—has to be perfect, or the entire meal is flawed.

### Business Reality

Right now:
* I manage **3** dinner services and **1** small pop-up event per month, serving approximately **30 to 40** covers per sitting.
* My revenue model is highly variable, averaging between **$18,000 and $22,000** in weekly gross revenue, with a tight focus on maintaining a Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) under 30%.
* My team is small but intensely specialized: **1 Sous Chef, 2 Line Cooks, 1 Prep Cook, and 1 FOH Manager.**
* My geographic scope is hyper-localized, centered within the historic district, with most of my critical suppliers located at the Saturday morning farmer's market.
* My current tech setup is a chaotic mix: **Google Sheets** for costing and inventory, **physical reservation books/notebooks** for reservations, **WhatsApp groups** for last-minute supplier communication, and **dedicated email chains** for vendor invoices.

---

### Primary Failure: The Ingredient Gap

My biggest operational failure isn't in scheduling or booking; it’s the gap between *availability* and *menu design*. If a farmer calls me—or better yet, shows up at the market—with a phenomenal haul of purple heirloom tomatoes that I didn't know I could use, I have to fundamentally redesign the menu, adjust the entire prep list, and recalculate the costing *immediately*. Because my current system is structured around static menus and predictable inventory, I cannot dynamically model the impact of a spontaneous, perfect ingredient arrival. This forces me to either pass up the best product of the season or spend valuable hours manually cross-referencing recipes and costs, which costs me time, reputation, and ultimately, money I could spend on the next perfect haul.

---

### Structural Issue: Variable Sourcing Integration

My suppliers operate on a biological schedule, not a business one. I don't order "20 pounds of carrots"; I order "whatever is ripe and beautiful from Farmer John’s patch this week." My current workaround is a mix of **personal phone calls and handwritten notes** that I must track manually. When the sheet is updated, I have to remember to cross-reference that change against the menu board, the prep list, *and* the allocated labor cost. This is where things break: if I forget to update the menu board after a last-minute ingredient swap, I risk confusing my staff and, worse, over-committing to a portion size I don't have the ingredients for, leading to waste and disappointing my diners.

---

### Structural Issue: Dynamic Recipe Adaptation

My recipes are not fixed; they are *guidelines*. When a key ingredient changes (e.g., the local fish catch changes from cod to rockfish), I have to manually draft a completely new recipe card, update the component costing (which is based on the day's market price, not a wholesale average), and then ensure the prep cook understands the new technique. I use **a mix of printed recipe binders and cloud-synced Google Docs**, which are useless for this. The moment I have to adjust a recipe based on a variable, high-quality ingredient, the system breaks because it cannot model the *directional* cost impact (e.g., "if we use this $15/lb local cheese, the cost per plate increases by 8%, but the perceived value increases by 20%").

---

### Structural Issue: Labor Allocation to Hyper-Local Prep

My prep staff's tasks are dictated by what the farmers bring in that day, not by a standard weekly schedule. For example, if I get 50 pounds of specialty peppers, the prep cook has to dedicate an unexpected, high-labor block to cleaning, sorting, and prepping those specific peppers, which then affects who is available to tackle the standard tasks. My workaround is a **whiteboard and a chaotic series of texts** to the Sous Chef. This is a massive bottleneck. I cannot accurately track the labor hours required for specialized, unpredictable, high-volume prep tasks and assign them correctly against the day's projected revenue, leading to poor labor forecasting and wasted staff time waiting for the next ingredient drop.

---

### Structural Issue: Non-Standard Inventory Tracking

I don't buy ingredients in standard wholesale units. I buy "a basket of green beans" or "a crate of heirloom tomatoes." These units are subjective and highly variable in weight and volume. My inventory system is **a messy spreadsheet that requires manual input every time a delivery arrives**. When I calculate my theoretical inventory usage, I have to estimate the weight of a "basket," which is always inaccurate. This means I constantly over-order or under-order, and I have no central, auditable record of the actual physical usage of my most valuable, unique, non-standardized ingredients, making waste tracking nearly impossible.

---

### Psychological Model

I optimize for: **Culinary integrity and the narrative connection between the plate and the place.**
I refuse to compromise on: **The provenance of every single ingredient.**
I avoid: **Anything that feels like it's optimized for scale or generic efficiency; I hate the feeling of being managed by a number.**
I evaluate tools by: **How flexible they are when a beautiful, spontaneous ingredient disrupts the entire plan.**

I operate on gut feeling and deep, personal relationships. My processes are messy because the best ingredients are messy. If a system forces me into rigid predictability, I will abandon it. I need a tool that acts as a smart assistant, not a manager.

---

### Pass / Fail Conditions

For this system to work for me, it must:

1. The system must allow me to define and prioritize ingredient sourcing based on hyper-local supplier profiles, not just commodity pricing.
2. The system must support dynamic menu generation that automatically flags and recalculates COGS and prep labor when a key ingredient is swapped out or added spontaneously.
3. The system must handle inventory units that are qualitative and variable (e.g., "bunch," "basket," "handful") and track their estimated weight/yield upon receipt.
4. The system must centralize communication and resource allocation for specialized, non-standard prep tasks, linking ingredient arrival directly to necessary labor hours.
5. The system must allow me to create and track bespoke, small-group bookings (e.g., a party of 8 for a private tasting) that require a unique, non-standard menu build and specialized staffing.
6. The system must provide a transparent audit trail that shows which specific supplier provided which unique ingredient, linking it directly to the final dish's story and plate costing.
7. The system must allow me to model ingredient cost fluctuations based on negotiated, non-standard pricing (e.g., "Supplier X charges $12/lb today, but $15/lb next week").
