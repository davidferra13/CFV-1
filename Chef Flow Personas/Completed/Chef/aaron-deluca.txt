**Chef Profile: "Aaron Deluca" - Private Chef (Solo-Strained)**

I don’t need a brigade. I don’t need a full catering team. I just need *one* reliable person at the right time—and I can never find them. Sometimes it’s another chef who understands pacing. Sometimes it’s just someone to pass plates. That gap is what breaks my events.

### Business Reality

Right now:

* 6–10 private dinners per month (2–12 guests, mostly in-home)
* $12,000–$28,000 monthly revenue
* Solo operator 90% of the time
* 1–2 events per week where I *should* have help but don’t
* 10–20 outreach attempts per month to find assistants (texts, DMs, word of mouth)
* ~30–50% of events executed understaffed
* 2–4 service breakdown moments per month (timing slips, plating delays, poor flow)

### Primary Failure: Inconsistent, Last-Minute Staffing

I don’t have a reliable bench. When I need help, I scramble—texting people, posting, asking around. Sometimes I get someone inexperienced, sometimes no one at all. There’s no predictability. That means I either overwork myself or risk bringing in someone who slows me down. My workaround is just doing everything myself, but that caps the level of service I can deliver.

### Structural Issue: No Lightweight Hiring Layer

Every staffing option feels too heavy. Agencies are overkill for a 6-person dinner. Platforms are built for large events or full teams. I just need one competent person for 3–5 hours. There’s no system designed for small, precise staffing needs. My workaround is informal networks, but they’re unreliable.

### Structural Issue: Skill Mismatch

Even when I find someone, they’re often not aligned with what I need. A line cook doesn’t necessarily know how to work in a private home. A server might not understand timing or plating expectations. I spend time explaining basics instead of executing. My workaround is lowering expectations, which affects quality.

### Structural Issue: Trust & Accountability Gap

I’m inviting someone into a client’s home. That requires trust. But I rarely have verified information about who I’m bringing in—their reliability, their behavior, their experience. If they don’t show up or perform poorly, I take the hit. My workaround is working with the same few people when possible, but availability is inconsistent.

### Structural Issue: Timing & Availability Friction

Private dinners are often last-minute or irregular—weekends, odd hours, short notice. Most people aren’t available when I need them. There’s no system that matches my exact timing needs with someone ready to work. My workaround is adjusting my schedule or declining opportunities, which limits growth.

### Psychological Model

I optimize for: reliability and minimal friction in getting help
I refuse to compromise on: maintaining control of service quality
I avoid: overcomplicated hiring processes and unreliable people
I evaluate tools by: whether they get me the right person, at the right time, with no hassle

### Pass / Fail Conditions

For this system to work for me, it must:

1. Allow me to find and book a single helper (chef or server) quickly for small events.
2. Match me with people who have relevant experience in private dining environments.
3. Provide verified profiles with reliability indicators (attendance, reviews, history).
4. Handle scheduling so I can secure help even on short notice.
5. Clearly define roles so the person knows exactly what’s expected before arriving.
6. Ensure accountability—if someone cancels or underperforms, there’s a fallback or consequence.
7. Require minimal setup so I can use it in real time without planning far in advance.
