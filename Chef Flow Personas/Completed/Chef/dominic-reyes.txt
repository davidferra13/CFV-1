**Chef Profile: "Dominic Reyes" - Chef-Owner (Displaced)**

I built my reputation on cooking, not managing a business. Now that I’ve started my own operation, I barely touch the stove. My days are filled with logistics, emails, pricing, scheduling, and problem-solving—everything except the thing I’m actually good at.

### Business Reality

Right now:

* 10–15 events per month (private dinners, small events, occasional corporate work)
* $35,000–$75,000 monthly revenue
* Team of 3–5 (prep cooks, servers, part-time help)
* 60–80% of my time spent on admin, coordination, and client management
* 100+ emails/messages per week across text, email, and social platforms
* Menus, pricing, sourcing, and staffing all managed manually
* Cooking personally at only 30–40% of events

### Primary Failure: Operator Overload

I became the bottleneck for everything. Every inquiry, decision, change, and issue flows through me. I write menus, respond to clients, calculate pricing, order ingredients, coordinate staff, and troubleshoot problems. By the time I get to the kitchen, I’m already drained. The business is growing, but my role has shifted from chef to full-time operator. The cost is that my core skill—cooking—is being diluted. My workaround is working longer hours, but that just spreads me thinner.

### Structural Issue: No Delegation Framework

I have help, but I don’t have systems. Tasks live in my head or scattered across messages, so I can’t hand them off cleanly. If I delegate, I still have to explain everything in detail and double-check it afterward. It’s faster to just do it myself, which defeats the purpose. My workaround is partial delegation, but it creates inconsistency and still keeps me overloaded.

### Structural Issue: Fragmented Workflow

Client communication, menu creation, sourcing, scheduling, and execution all happen in different places. Nothing flows together. I’ll get an inquiry via text, build a menu in a document, calculate pricing in my head or a spreadsheet, then order ingredients manually. Every step is disconnected, and I have to carry context between them. My workaround is managing it mentally, but that doesn’t scale.

### Structural Issue: Loss of Craft Time

Cooking is no longer the default—it’s the exception. Even when I’m in the kitchen, I’m thinking about emails I haven’t answered or problems I need to solve. There’s no protected space for the actual craft. My workaround is blocking off time to cook, but it’s constantly interrupted by business demands.

### Psychological Model

I optimize for: getting things done quickly and keeping the business moving
I refuse to compromise on: the quality of the food and client experience
I avoid: unnecessary complexity and systems that require heavy setup
I evaluate tools by: whether they give me back time to actually cook

### Pass / Fail Conditions

For this system to work for me, it must:

1. Automatically capture and structure client inquiries so I don’t have to manage them manually.
2. Turn inquiries into menus, pricing, and plans without requiring me to rebuild everything from scratch.
3. Allow me to delegate tasks clearly with full context so my team can execute without constant oversight.
4. Connect all parts of the workflow (clients, menus, sourcing, scheduling) into a single system.
5. Reduce the volume of manual communication I have to handle daily.
6. Give me clear visibility into operations without requiring me to be involved in every step.
7. Return a significant portion of my time back to cooking without sacrificing control.
