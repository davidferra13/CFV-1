**Chef Profile: "Evan Morales" - Private Chef (Overloader)**

I don’t just forget things—I overload myself the second I see them. Every message turns into five mental tasks: reply, plan, source, confirm, follow up. I don’t write any of it down because I think I’ll handle it “in a second.” Then I move on, stack more on top, and everything collapses.

### Business Reality

Right now:

* 8–13 events per month (private dinners + repeat clients)
* $18,000–$38,000 monthly revenue
* Solo operator with occasional server/prep help
* 70–140 inbound messages per week across SMS, email, and social
* Average response delay: 6–36 hours depending on backlog
* 15–30 active “in-progress” conversations at any given time
* 8–20 dropped or mishandled details per week (timing, counts, dietary notes, follow-ups)

### Primary Failure: Mental Task Explosion

Every message becomes a mini project in my head. A simple inquiry turns into menu ideas, pricing calculations, ingredient sourcing, scheduling conflicts, and staffing—all at once. I try to hold it mentally instead of externalizing it. By the time I circle back, I’ve either forgotten key details or I’m too overwhelmed to start. My workaround is jumping back into the thread and reconstructing everything, but that’s slow and often incomplete.

### Structural Issue: No Decomposition of Work

I treat everything as a single blob instead of breaking it into steps. “Respond to client” actually means: confirm details, propose menu, price it, check availability, and follow up. Because it’s not broken down, I procrastinate the entire thing. My workaround is waiting for a block of time to “handle it all,” which rarely exists.

### Structural Issue: Context Switching Penalty

I’m constantly switching between conversations, each with different requirements. One client needs a quote, another needs a menu revision, another needs confirmation. There’s no system preserving context, so every time I return to a thread, I have to reload everything mentally. My workaround is rereading entire conversations, which wastes time and drains focus.

### Structural Issue: No “Done” State

Even when I reply, I’m not actually done. There’s always a next step—waiting on confirmation, sending a revision, following up. But once I’ve sent a message, I mentally check it off. There’s no persistent state tracking what’s still pending. My workaround is relying on the other person to reply, which means I lose control of the timeline.

### Psychological Model

I optimize for: clearing things quickly and reducing immediate pressure
I refuse to compromise on: delivering accurate, thoughtful responses
I avoid: partially completed work and unclear next steps
I evaluate tools by: whether they reduce mental load and break work into manageable pieces

### Pass / Fail Conditions

For this system to work for me, it must:

1. Automatically break incoming requests into discrete, actionable steps (reply, quote, confirm, follow-up).
2. Preserve full context for each conversation so I never have to reconstruct it manually.
3. Track the state of every interaction (new, in progress, waiting, completed) beyond just “replied.”
4. Surface the exact next action required for each item so I don’t have to think about it.
5. Allow me to act on individual steps without needing to complete the entire workflow at once.
6. Maintain visibility into all active work so nothing gets buried under new messages.
7. Prompt follow-ups proactively when a conversation stalls, instead of waiting for the other person.
