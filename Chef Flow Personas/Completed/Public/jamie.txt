**Public Profile: "Jamie" Rosenthal - Local Culinary Curator (Experience Hunter)**

I live for the moment when I find a hidden gem—a chef or a concept that tells a truly unique story, especially when it’s popping up for a limited time. I spend my time scouting these micro-events, finding the next big thing before the mainstream media catches on. I need to curate a week's worth of amazing, varied food experiences that feel authentic and genuinely local, not just another corporate dinner.

### Business Reality

Right now:
* I search for or compare **7 to 10** chefs, events, cuisines, or menus per **week**
* My typical ticket price range is **$150 to $400** per person, and I manage bookings for groups of **8 to 150** guests per event.
* I need hyper-local, high-concept events, preferably within a **15-mile radius** of a major urban center, with a focus on seasonal, unique, or ethnic cuisines.
* My inquiry context is always a **media booking/feature story**, requiring me to vet the chef's operational capability, the menu's story, and the logistics for a public audience.
* My current discovery setup is a fragmented mess: **Instagram DMs**, **Google Maps searches**, **Scattered local food blogs**, and **Excel spreadsheets** I manually update every Friday.

---

### Primary Failure: The Story-to-Service Gap

The biggest operational problem is that the discovery process I rely on is purely aesthetic and anecdotal. I can find a chef who looks amazing on Instagram and has a beautiful photo of their dish, but I have no verifiable, aggregated data on their *operational capacity* for a pop-up. For example, I might love a chef's concept, but if they can only handle 20 guests a night and their prep kitchen is only available 4 days a week, I don't know that until I send 3-4 separate emails and get vague answers. This uncertainty forces me to significantly over-book my initial research time, often spending 6+ hours a week just confirming basic availability and scale, which severely limits how many high-potential concepts I can vet before my deadline.

---

### Structural Issue: Vetting Operational Capacity vs. Concept Appeal

I find it incredibly difficult to compare a chef's *artistic concept* against their *practical, scalable capacity*. If a chef posts a beautiful menu of 10 courses, I need to know immediately: Is this menu possible with their current equipment? Are they staffed to execute this volume for 150 people? My current workaround is calling them directly and asking a series of highly technical, open-ended questions that require them to switch from 'creative chef mode' to 'operations manager mode.' This often leads to vague, unquantifiable answers that I have to manually translate into risk assessments.

---

### Structural Issue: Tracking Multi-Stage Pop-Up Logistics

Pop-ups are transient, meaning their logistics change constantly (e.g., "We're moving the pop-up from the back patio to the warehouse parking lot if it rains"). I need a centralized view of the *physical and logistical constraints* for the entire event week. I currently manage this using a combination of Google Docs and text message chains with my assistant, which means if one chef changes their power requirements (e.g., needing three dedicated high-amperage outlets instead of one), I have to manually cross-reference that against the venue's capacity plan, risking a massive, last-minute operational failure.

---

### Structural Issue: Contextualizing Public Interest and Media Appeal

I need to understand how a chef’s *story* translates into a public experience, which is different from just viewing a menu. I want to see aggregated data points like, "This chef has successfully run 12 pop-ups in the last 18 months, averaging a 4.8/5 star rating from 800+ guests," or "This concept has been featured in 5 major local publications." Right now, I have to pull this data from disparate sources (Yelp, Google Reviews, local news archives, my own contacts), which is time-consuming, incomplete, and makes it impossible to quickly compare the *market viability* of two equally talented chefs.

---

### Structural Issue: Managing the Booking Trust and Inquiry Funnel

The process of getting a public to commit to a high-ticket pop-up experience is fragile. I need a system that manages the pre-booking inquiry funnel, allowing me to qualify the interest of potential attendees (e.g., "Are you interested in the high-end tasting menu, or the casual street food option?"). Currently, I am forced to use a combination of email forms and phone calls, which means I lose the conversational context of their initial interest. I can't track if they were initially drawn to the chef's backstory, the unique cocktail pairing, or the specific cuisine, making it impossible to optimize my promotion for future events.

---

### Psychological Model

I optimize for: **Novelty, Storytelling, and Proven Operational Excellence.**
I refuse to compromise on: **The perceived scarcity and high quality of the experience.**
I avoid: **Bureaucratic, opaque, or overly technical interfaces.**

I think like a curator: I am always looking for the intersection of *uniqueness* and *feasibility*. I am highly visual and process-driven; if the data presentation isn't immediately scannable, color-coded, and narrative-rich, I will abandon it. I evaluate tools by how quickly they allow me to build a compelling, trustworthy narrative for a third party (the consumer) while simultaneously giving me a clear, real-time view of the chef's operational constraints.

---

### Pass / Fail Conditions

For this system to work for me, it must:

1. The system must allow me to filter chefs based on **verified operational metrics** (e.g., maximum simultaneous cover count, required kitchen equipment, and average weekly prep time).
2. The system must provide a **unified, time-series view** of a chef’s booking history, showing which specific date ranges and days of the week they have already booked or are unavailable for the next 90 days.
3. The system must allow me to **overlay real-time venue constraints** (e.g., "max 100 guests," "outdoor seating only," "requires external generator access") directly onto a chef's proposed event profile.
4. The system must generate a **"Story Scorecard"** for a chef, compiling verifiable data points like "Number of featured media mentions," "Average guest rating from last 5 pop-ups," and "Total unique cuisines covered."
5. The system must allow me to **save and compare multiple competing pop-up concepts** side-by-side, showing their respective menus, pricing structures, and estimated staffing needs in a single view.
6. The system must provide a **public-facing, but customizable, booking inquiry form** that allows me to qualify the user's interest level (e.g., "Are you interested in the $150 tasting menu or the $50 street food pairing?") before the booking is confirmed.
7. The system must integrate a **dynamic scheduling calendar** that shows available dates and automatically flags potential conflicts based on the chef's stated capacity limits, preventing me from proposing an impossible event.
