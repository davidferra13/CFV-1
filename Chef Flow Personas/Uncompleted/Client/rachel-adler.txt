Client Profile: "Rachel Adler" - UX Designer, Consumer Apps (Friction Killer)

I design flows for a living. Every extra field, every unnecessary tap - I see it immediately. If your payment experience isn’t effortless, I assume the rest of the product is just as sloppy.

Business Reality
Right now:

I book 4–8 private dining experiences per year, usually 2–8 guests.

Budget ranges from $1,200 to $5,500. I’m not cost-sensitive, but I am friction-sensitive.

I expect to go from decision to confirmation in under 2 minutes.

Dietary needs are simple but should be remembered across events.

Locations vary, but I expect that to be abstracted away in the system.

I use both mobile and desktop, but mobile is primary for final actions.

I rely heavily on saved payment methods and autofill ecosystems.

Primary Failure: Payment Friction Overload
The biggest issue is when payment feels like work. Typing information, confirming multiple times, dealing with unclear states - it’s exhausting. I’ve abandoned bookings at the payment step more than anywhere else. If I’m ready to pay and you slow me down, you lose me.

Structural Issue: No Progressive Disclosure
Payment flows often dump everything at once - billing address, card info, confirmation screens. That’s bad design. I want progressive disclosure: show me only what’s necessary, when it’s necessary. Wallets solve this by abstracting everything into one action. If you’re not using that, you’re forcing unnecessary cognitive load.

Structural Issue: Lack of Trust Signals
Manual forms feel less secure than wallet-based payments. I trust Apple Pay and Google Pay because they’re standardized and tokenized. When I see a custom form, I question its security. That hesitation is enough to stop me.

Structural Issue: Redundant Confirmation Steps
I don’t need three confirmation screens. If I tap “Pay” and authenticate, that should be it. Extra confirmations feel like the system doesn’t trust itself. That’s a bad signal.

Psychological Model
I optimize for: minimal interaction and maximum confidence
I refuse to compromise on: effortless, intuitive payment flows
I avoid: redundant steps, manual input, and unclear states
I evaluate tools by: how invisible the payment process feels

Pass / Fail Conditions
For this system to work for me, it must:

Default to wallet-based payments with zero manual entry as the primary flow.

Minimize the number of steps from pricing to payment - ideally one action plus authentication.

Use progressive disclosure to only show necessary information at each step.

Eliminate redundant confirmation screens - payment should complete after a single verified action.

Maintain clear, consistent UI states so I always know where I am in the process.

Reinforce trust with secure, recognizable payment methods rather than custom forms.

Allow me to complete the entire booking without friction or hesitation at the payment stage.
