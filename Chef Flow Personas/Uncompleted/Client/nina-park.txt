Client Profile: "Nina Park" - Data Analyst, Consumer Insights (Clarity)

If I can’t understand the number immediately, I don’t trust it. I need clarity, not just accuracy.

Business Reality
Right now:

I book 3–5 private dinners per year, usually 2–7 guests.

Budget ranges from $1,000 to $4,000.

I expect to understand pricing at a glance.

Dietary needs vary but must be reflected clearly.

Locations vary but should not complicate pricing.

I prefer clean, readable interfaces.

I value clarity over complexity.

Primary Failure: Unclear Pricing Presentation
The biggest issue is when pricing is technically correct but visually confusing. Too many numbers, unclear breakdowns, or hidden components make it hard to trust. I shouldn’t have to interpret the price.

Structural Issue: Poor Information Hierarchy
Important numbers are buried or overshadowed by less relevant details. I want the total, then the breakdown - not the other way around.

Structural Issue: Overloaded UI
Too much information at once creates noise. I want optional detail, not forced complexity.

Structural Issue: Inconsistent Formatting
Different formats across screens create confusion - sometimes per person, sometimes total, sometimes both.

Psychological Model
I optimize for: clarity and readability
I refuse to compromise on: instantly understandable pricing
I avoid: cluttered or inconsistent displays
I evaluate tools by: how quickly I can interpret the price

Pass / Fail Conditions
For this system to work for me, it must:

Present total pricing clearly and prominently at all times.

Provide a clean, optional breakdown that’s easy to expand or collapse.

Maintain consistent formatting across all views.

Avoid clutter while still offering full transparency.

Highlight key cost drivers without overwhelming detail.

Ensure pricing is readable at a glance.

Support quick comprehension without interpretation effort.
