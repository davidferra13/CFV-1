Client Profile: "Tanya Brooks" - Emergency Room Nurse (Hands-Off)

I don’t have time to learn a system or manage anything. I just want to say “I need a chef,” give my address and basic info, and have someone competent reach out and handle the rest.

Business Reality
Right now:

I book 2–4 private dinners a year - usually after a long stretch of work or for a small celebration with 3–6 people.

Budget is $800–$2,500. I’m flexible if it means I don’t have to think about anything.

I’m not splitting payments or coordinating a group. I pay, maybe one other person chips in, but that’s it.

Dietary needs are simple but important - my sister is vegetarian, I don’t eat pork. I’ll mention it once.

Events are always at my apartment. Small kitchen, nothing fancy. I assume the chef figures it out.

My schedule is unpredictable. I might submit a request at 2am after a shift and expect someone to follow up later.

I use my phone for everything. I don’t want to download apps, log in, or manage dashboards.

Primary Failure: Forced Engagement
The biggest problem is being forced to participate in a process I don’t care about. Forms, profiles, menu selection, messaging threads - it’s too much. I don’t want to browse chefs, compare options, or make decisions beyond the basics. I’ve abandoned bookings because it felt like I was being turned into the coordinator instead of the client. I’m paying to offload the thinking, not to do more of it.

Structural Issue: No “Set and Forget” Flow
There’s no true one-step submission. Everything branches into more steps - confirm this, choose that, review options. I want to submit once and be done until someone reaches out with something real. Right now, I feel like I have to stay engaged the entire time or the booking stalls.

Structural Issue: Too Many Decisions Upfront
I’m asked to pick menus, styles, preferences before I even know who I’m working with. I don’t have the energy for that. I’d rather a chef come to me with a clear proposal after understanding the basics. When I’m forced to decide early, I either rush it or stop altogether.

Structural Issue: No Controlled Follow-Up
When I do submit something, responses come in inconsistently - multiple chefs, different formats, unclear next steps. It becomes another thing to manage. I don’t want options overload; I want a clean handoff where one qualified person takes over and guides the rest.

Psychological Model
I optimize for: minimal effort and zero ongoing involvement
I refuse to compromise on: not having to manage the process
I avoid: forms, browsing, comparisons, and extended communication
I evaluate tools by: how little I have to interact after the first step

Pass / Fail Conditions
For this system to work for me, it must:

Allow me to submit a request with only essential fields (address, date, headcount, basic notes) in under 30 seconds.

Not require account creation, profile setup, or app download at any point.

Route my request to a single qualified chef (or tightly filtered match) instead of sending me multiple options to compare.

Ensure that the chef initiates contact with a clear proposal so I don’t have to chase or manage responses.

Avoid asking me for additional decisions until a chef has engaged and is guiding the process.
