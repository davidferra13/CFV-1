Client Profile: "Maya Ellison" - Brand Strategist & Social Host (Curator)

I don’t want to pick from three prebuilt menus. That feels lazy and boxed in. I want to build something that fits my group exactly, even if that means making a hundred small decisions.

Business Reality
Right now:

I host 6–10 group dinners per year, usually 6–12 guests. These are social, aesthetic, and very intentional - people expect a certain vibe.

Budgets range from $2,500 to $7,500. I’m willing to spend more if the experience feels tailored.

Payments are usually split, but I’ll front the deposit ($800–$2,000) to lock things in.

My groups are opinionated. Some want lighter food, some want indulgent, some care about presentation more than taste, some are picky but won’t say it directly.

Dietary restrictions vary every time - vegetarian, gluten-free, no pork, etc. - and I need flexibility without sacrificing the overall menu.

Locations are mostly Airbnbs or apartments with decent kitchens, but I don’t want to think about limitations unless they actually matter.

I coordinate everything through group chats, saved Instagram posts, and notes. It’s messy but visual.

Primary Failure: Forced Menu Boxes
The biggest issue is being forced to choose from fixed menus. I’ll get three options - Menu A, B, or C - and none of them fully work. One has a great starter but a boring main. Another has a good dessert but doesn’t fit dietary needs. I end up compromising instead of curating. It feels like I’m buying a preset package instead of creating something for my group. That kills the whole point of hiring a private chef.

Structural Issue: No Modular Menu System
There’s no system that lets me mix and match. I want to see a range of options - starters, mains, desserts - and build my own flow. Right now, if I ask to swap one dish, it turns into a custom request that slows everything down. I don’t understand why this isn’t standard. It should be as simple as selecting components.

Structural Issue: Group Input Chaos
When I involve my friends, it gets messy. I send menu options, people respond in different formats - “I like this,” “not that,” “anything but mushrooms.” There’s no clean way to gather preferences and turn them into a decision. I end up interpreting everything manually, which leads to second-guessing and sometimes complaints after the fact.

Structural Issue: No Constraint Awareness
I understand chefs need constraints - cost, prep time, kitchen limitations - but I can’t see them. So I suggest combinations that might not work, and then I get pushback later. It makes the process feel like trial and error. I want to explore options within real boundaries, not guess blindly.

Psychological Model
I optimize for: flexibility, personalization, and group satisfaction
I refuse to compromise on: having control over the final menu composition
I avoid: preset packages and rigid choices
I evaluate tools by: how freely I can explore options without breaking the system

Pass / Fail Conditions
For this system to work for me, it must:

Present a wide range of individual dish options (e.g., 15–25 items) organized by course, not just fixed menus.

Allow me to build a custom multi-course menu by selecting individual dishes, with instant feedback on compatibility and flow.

Show real-time constraints (pricing, prep complexity, dietary conflicts) as I build, so I don’t choose combinations that won’t work.

Enable my group to view options and submit preferences or votes in a structured way, without messy chat responses.

Automatically adapt the menu to accommodate dietary restrictions while preserving the overall concept.

Update total pricing dynamically as I add or swap dishes, so I always know where I stand.

Let the chef review and approve the constructed menu with minimal back-and-forth, rather than restarting the process.
