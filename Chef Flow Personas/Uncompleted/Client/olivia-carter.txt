Client Profile: "Olivia Carter" - Marketing Manager & Social Host (Novice)

I want to do something different for my friends - a cooking class at my house - but I have no idea how this actually works. I don’t know what I’m supposed to provide, what the chef brings, or how pricing is even calculated. I just know I want it to happen.

Business Reality
Right now:

I’m planning 1–2 cooking class-style events per year, usually 5–8 people. This is more about the experience than just eating.

Budget is loosely $1,000–$3,000, but I don’t know if that’s realistic because I’ve never done this before.

I assume I need to pay a deposit, but I don’t know what’s standard or what I’m paying for - ingredients, instruction, time, cleanup?

Guest expectations vary - some want to actually cook, others just want to watch and drink wine. I don’t know how to balance that.

Dietary restrictions exist but are simple - one vegetarian, maybe one gluten-free. I don’t know how that affects what we can cook.

The event is at my house. I have a normal kitchen, but I don’t know if it’s “good enough” or if I need extra equipment.

I currently just Google things, maybe message a chef on Instagram, and hope for the best. There’s no structure.

Primary Failure: Total Uncertainty
The biggest problem is that I don’t even know what questions to ask. I don’t know what a cooking class setup requires, so I can’t give good information, and I can’t evaluate what a chef tells me. I’ve had conversations where chefs ask me things like “what equipment do you have?” or “what format do you want?” and I freeze. I don’t want to guess wrong and mess up the event. That uncertainty makes me hesitate to even book.

Structural Issue: No Guided Setup
There’s no step-by-step guidance for someone doing this for the first time. I’m expected to define the experience - hands-on vs demo, menu complexity, timing - without knowing what those choices actually mean. My workaround is asking vague questions and hoping the chef interprets them correctly, which isn’t reliable.

Structural Issue: Equipment Blind Spots
I don’t know what equipment is required. Do I need extra cutting boards? Knives? Portable burners? Enough space for everyone to participate? I don’t want to overbuy or underprepare. Right now, I either assume my kitchen is fine or stress about everything that could go wrong.

Structural Issue: Pricing Confusion
I don’t understand how pricing works for a cooking class. Is it per person? Per hour? Based on the menu? What costs extra? When I get a quote, I can’t tell if it’s fair or if I’m missing something. That makes me hesitant to commit, even if I like the chef.

Psychological Model
I optimize for: clarity and confidence in what I’m booking
I refuse to compromise on: not feeling confused or unprepared
I avoid: guessing, vague answers, and unclear pricing
I evaluate tools by: how well they guide me through something I’ve never done before

Pass / Fail Conditions
For this system to work for me, it must:

Provide a guided setup flow that explains each decision (class format, menu style, participation level) in simple terms as I go.

Ask me about my kitchen and automatically assess whether it’s sufficient, including identifying any missing equipment.

Clearly define what the chef will bring versus what I need to provide, without me having to guess.

Offer sample class formats (e.g., hands-on, demo, hybrid) with clear explanations so I can choose confidently.

Generate transparent pricing that shows exactly what I’m paying for (instruction time, ingredients, equipment, cleanup).

Adjust recommendations based on my guest count, dietary needs, and space limitations automatically.

Give me a final, simple plan that confirms everything - what’s happening, what’s needed, and what’s covered - so I feel fully prepared going into the event.
