**Guest Profile: "Olajide Olatunji" - High-Profile Creator/Entertainer (Demand Driven)**

I am Olajide. My life revolves around massive scale—whether it's a stadium show, a major brand deal, or simply maintaining my content schedule. I'm used to dealing with high volumes of people, rapid turnaround times, and absolute zero tolerance for operational hiccups. When I commit to an event, I expect it to run with the same flawless efficiency I bring to my own projects.

### Business Reality

Right now:
* I attend approximately **6-8** private dining, farm dinner, or ticketed events per quarter.
* For these events, I typically spend **$2,500 - $5,000** per person on tickets and associated experiences, excluding accommodation.
* I have multiple severe dietary restrictions (gluten, specific nuts, high-fructose corn syrup avoidance) and significant accessibility needs (requiring ground-floor access and specific seating arrangements for movement).
* My event context is usually a high-profile, semi-private gathering of **12-25** people at a trendy, high-demand venue, often on a Friday or Saturday night, and the event is usually tied to a launch or celebration.
* My current communication setup is a chaotic mix: initial booking via email, dietary restrictions sent via a separate text message to the organizer, seating preferences are discussed over a follow-up Zoom call, and finally, I receive a PDF itinerary 48 hours before the event.

---

### Primary Failure: Inconsistent Communication Cascade

The biggest operational problem is the lack of a single source of truth for my participation. Because I am a high-profile guest, my expectations for communication are enormous, and the current process forces me to become a project manager for my own dinner. If the dietary details (which are critical for my health) are sent via text, but the seating chart is in an email, and the final menu changes are announced on Instagram stories, I risk miscommunication. This creates a massive risk of service failure—meaning the kitchen is unprepared for my specific needs—which costs me not just my time, but my reputation and my health.

---

### Structural Issue: Manual Data Hand-offs

The system forces me to manually re-input or re-confirm my details across multiple platforms. I have to email my dietary restrictions to the booking agent, and then I have to repeat them to the event coordinator who is responsible for the kitchen. This process means that crucial data points (like "no nuts, no gluten, must be ground floor") are easily dropped or misinterpreted during the hand-off. The workaround is always calling the organizer three times just to confirm the core details, which is a massive drain on my time.

---

### Structural Issue: Uncentralized Event Documentation

All the information about my attendance—from the original booking contract to the final menu tasting notes to the post-event feedback survey—is scattered across 3-5 different documents or folders. I cannot access a single place to see the complete lifecycle of the event. If I need to reference the initial agreement details (e.g., the guaranteed minimum spend or the contracted headcount) for a follow-up, I have to ask the venue staff to dig through their archives. This lack of central documentation makes follow-up planning incredibly difficult.

---

### Structural Issue: Reactive Seating and Accessibility Planning

Seating and accessibility are not handled until 24-48 hours before the event, which is too late for a high-stakes gathering. I have to verbally communicate my specific physical needs (e.g., need for a specific pathway clearance or proximity to a restroom) to a human being. If the venue staff or the event planner are not immediately aware of this, it means the physical layout of the event is optimized for the general population, not for my specific needs, creating a significant inconvenience and potential hazard.

---

### Structural Issue: Disconnected Feedback Loop

My feedback is critical because my presence elevates the perceived status of the event. However, the feedback mechanism is always an afterthought—a survey emailed 3 days after the event. This is too late, and the feedback is disconnected from the operational details. I want to provide feedback on *why* the service failed (e.g., "The appetizer was served 15 minutes late, which impacted the flow of conversation"), not just a star rating. The current process forces me to generalize my critique, diluting its value for the venue.

---

### Psychological Model

I optimize for: **Flawless, seamless execution and predictability.**
I refuse to compromise on: **Personal health requirements and the perceived exclusivity of the experience.**
I avoid: **Repetitive data entry, ambiguous communication, and anything that feels disorganized or ad-hoc.**
I evaluate tools by: **How quickly they reduce friction and save me time on logistics.**

I think in terms of scalable systems. If a process requires me to manually coordinate more than two people, I assume it is inefficient. I expect the technology to handle the complexity so I can focus solely on the experience itself.

---

### Pass / Fail Conditions

For this system to work for me, it must:

1. The system must automatically flag and prioritize my dietary restrictions and accessibility needs across all staff views (booking, kitchen, service).
2. The system must provide a single, real-time communication dashboard that syncs confirmed RSVPs, menu changes, and seating assignments, visible to both me and the event organizer.
3. The system must allow me to upload and store all event-related documentation (contracts, menus, tasting notes) and display a chronological history of my participation.
4. The system must integrate my unique physical requirements into the physical layout planning view, alerting staff to necessary pathway clearances before the event day.
5. The system must allow me to submit detailed, actionable feedback linked specifically to time, dish, or staff member, allowing the venue to track performance metrics.
6. The system must provide me with a clear, multi-stage timeline (e.g., 6 weeks out, 1 week out, 24 hours out) detailing what I need to do and what the venue is doing.
7. The system must allow the kitchen to receive immediate, pre-approved modifications to the menu based on my profile, generating an automatic, costed prep list for the staff.
